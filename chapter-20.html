Northbound

Chapter Twenty

Donal

The track came down out of the hills onto the head of a sea-loch in the middle of the afternoon. There was a small bay with a stone jetty and a cluster of half a dozen white-washed houses set back from the water, and beyond the houses the great long blue water of the loch running west toward the open sea. The mountains stood on either side of the loch in their wet dark green. There was a thin smoke coming from the chimney of the second-to-last house. There was a small wooden boat tied up at the jetty with its mast lying across the thwarts. There were a great many gulls.

I stood at the top of the track for a long while looking down. I had a feeling, looking down at the bay, that I had seen it before. I had not. I had perhaps imagined it, in some early part of my walking, when I had been giving shape to the place I was walking toward. The imagined bay and the real bay had the same look. This was perhaps the only time in my life such a thing had happened to me, and I have thought about it since, and I cannot account for it. I will not try to.

The dog went down the track ahead of me. He was not limping. He had not limped since the Trossachs. He went down with his easy long stride toward the bay.

· · ·

The man at the second-to-last house came out as we walked along the front. He had been working on something at a table set up under a small lean-to at the side of the house, with his back to the road, and he straightened and turned as we came up. He was old. He was perhaps seventy, perhaps older. He had a long grey beard and pale blue eyes and he wore a thick fisherman's jersey over canvas trousers, and rubber boots, and a flat cap. His hands as he turned were holding a fid and a length of wet rope, and he set them down on the table without taking his eyes off me, and walked to the gate of his small front garden.

"You'll be wanting the island," he said.

I stopped. I looked at him.

"How do you know."

"It is what people come down this track for," he said. "It is the only thing they come for. I am the only one who can take them. I am Donal. I have a boat. You will be the first this year, although there has been one or two in the years before."

"Iona told me there would be a man."

"Iona," he said. He smiled in his beard. "I do not know an Iona. There is no need. We are a list, of a kind. The list keeps itself. Come in. You will eat. You will tell me about your dog, who is a fine dog. We will see when we can go."

· · ·

His kitchen was a small low kitchen with a stone-flagged floor and a peat fire on a stone hearth and a window above the sink that gave straight out onto the water. He set me at a small wooden table at the window. He gave me a bowl of fish stew with potatoes and a piece of dark bread, and a glass of water from a stone jug. He gave the dog a bowl of the same on the floor, fish-flesh in pieces with the bones taken out, and the dog ate it with the same grave attention he had given to the bee woman's broth.

Donal sat opposite me. He drank tea. He did not eat. He watched me with his pale eyes, not hostile, not curious, only the patient attentive look of a man who has learned over many years to take a great deal in by sitting and not speaking. I ate. I did not feel hurried.

"The boat," I said when I had finished.

"The boat is good," he said. "The boat is ready. The boat is not the difficulty."

"What is the difficulty."

"The crossing," he said. "The water at the mouth of this loch is not a water you cross any day. It is a water you cross on the days it allows. You will see, tomorrow, perhaps, perhaps not. There is a tide, there are two tides, there is a wind that comes in over the islands from the open sea, there are days when the going is difficult, there are days when it is impossible. We do not cross on those days. We wait for a day when the loch allows. It is not always quick to come. Sometimes I have waited a week. Once, two weeks. The loch does not consult me about its weather."

I nodded.

"You understand."

"Yes."

"Good. You will stay with me. There is a small back room. There is a bed. There is a stove. You will help with what I am doing. I am working on a net at the moment, and on a rope, and on a few small things on the boat. You will work with me on what you can. We will eat. We will wait. The loch will tell us when to go."

"Donal."

"Yes."

"You go to the island."

"I go to the island. Yes."

"Why."

He looked at me a long moment. He set his cup down. He pulled at his beard once, slowly, with one hand.

"I have a wife on the island," he said. "I have had a wife on the island for forty-one years. I cross to her once a fortnight when the loch allows. I bring her flour and salt. I bring her seed potatoes in the spring. I bring her two new books a year that I have found. She does not need me to come. She is not lonely. I think she is the least lonely person on this earth. But I come, because I want to come, and she does not mind, and the crossing is a thing I am for."

"She lives there alone."

"She lives there with the deer," he said. "And with the things she does. She is a maker of small things. She makes them out of wood and out of bone and out of stone. She makes them for the place she is in. She does not make them for anybody. They sit on the shore. The sea takes some. The wind takes some. The deer pay no attention to them. That is why she makes them, perhaps. That is why I cannot make them. I cannot make a thing for no one. She can. She always could. It is what set her apart from me when we first met, and what we built our marriage on, after a fashion, and what she lives by now."

"You stay here."

"I stay here. I have my boat. I have the loch. I have the rope-work and the net-work that is the work of a man with a boat. I cross to her on the right days. I come back on the right days. We have made a marriage out of the crossing. It is a long quiet marriage. I would not change a great deal of it. I have lost the things I wanted to lose and I have kept the things I wanted to keep. There are not many men who can say that. I do not say it boastfully. I say it as a piece of luck."

"And the thing that came," I said.

"Came here, yes," he said. "Came here as it came everywhere. I was on the boat. I was out beyond the point. I felt it come over the water like a long shadow, although there was no shadow. It came on the water and it sat with me on the boat. I did not stop rowing. I was not afraid. I had had hard things ask things of me before. The sea asks hard things of a man with a boat, regularly, and a man learns to listen and to answer or not answer. I listened. I understood. I rowed back to the jetty. I tied the boat up. I went into the kitchen. I drank a glass of water. I sat at this table. The thing was with me at the table. We sat. After a time I said to it, in my mind, no, I have a wife on the island. The thing did not argue. It went."

"That was all."

"That was all," he said. "It does not argue. It is not that kind of thing. It asks. You answer or you do not. If you answer, it takes you. If you do not, it goes. It takes itself elsewhere, perhaps. It does not press."

"The ones who answered."

"They went," he said. "I cannot say where. I have my own thoughts. My thoughts are not worth giving to you. You will have your own. Everyone in this country has had to grow their own. The thing did not leave us a teaching. It left us a room. We are each of us furnishing it as we can."

· · ·

That afternoon I sat with him under the lean-to and watched him work the rope. He had me hold one end of it for him. The rope was thick and rough, perhaps three-strand sisal, and he was splicing in a new length where it had worn. He did the work without looking at it, mostly. He looked out at the water as he worked. The fid moved in his hand without seeming to be guided. I watched his hands. I had known a few men in my old life with hands like his, and I had never been one of them, and I had never thought to learn from one of them. The work moved itself between his fingers. Strand under strand. Strand over strand. In and out. The rope became, over an hour, a single rope again, with the splice almost invisible in it, smooth and tight.

"There," he said. He set it on the table. He looked at me. "You will help me with the next one."

"I do not know how."

"That is why you will help. You will learn it by doing it badly. You will not learn it by watching me."

I learned it by doing it badly. I did it badly for two days. By the third day my splice was passable. By the fourth it was not bad. Donal did not praise. He took the rope when I was done, examined it, set it aside, gave me the next. He nodded once when the work was good enough. He cut and re-laid two of my early splices when they were not. He said only, "That one was not enough. Try again." Then, "Better. Go on."

I had not, in the building of glass, been taught anything by a man like Donal. I had been taught a great many things by men in suits, in rooms, with diagrams on screens. None of those things had stayed in me. The rope-work began to stay in me on the second day. By the fourth day my hands knew it without being told. My hands had not known anything in nineteen years, except where to put themselves on a keyboard. They were quicker learners than my head. I had not known how patiently they had been waiting.

The loch did not allow the crossing. We waited.

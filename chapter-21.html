Northbound

Chapter Twenty-One

Waiting

We waited eight days for the loch.

I had thought the wait would be hard. I had walked a hundred and sixty days to get to a small white house on a small bay where a man with a boat was the last barrier between me and the place I had been walking toward, and I had thought that the wait, when it came, would put me at odds with myself. I had thought I would pace. I had thought I would stand at the edge of the jetty and look out at the loch in the way of a man who is being kept from a thing he believes is his by right.

I did not. I sat with Donal at the table under the lean-to. I worked the rope. I learned the names of the parts of his boat, which were the names a fishing boat had had when fishing boats were made of wood and rope and oiled canvas, and which had a rough music to them: the gunwale, the thwart, the strake, the keelson, the painter. I learned to scrape the small barnacles from the lower hull with a flat blade and a steady hand. I learned how he liked his nets folded. I learned how to bait a long line. I cut bait for him in the mornings on a board on the jetty, the dog watching me from a distance with his polite professional attention, and Donal sat on a low stool beside me and showed me, without once taking the knife from me, how to make the cuts so the bait stayed on the hook in a sea-current and did not wash off.

I had not known, on the road, how much I had wanted to be useful in this way. I had wanted to be useful, in the building of glass, but the use I had been there had been of a kind that I had not been able to feel. The rope had a feel. The bait had a feel. The hull of the boat under my palm in the morning when I checked her for the night's damp had a feel. The dog leaning against my knee at the end of a working day had a feel. I had begun to live in the country of feel after a long time outside it, and it was good country, and I did not want to leave.

· · ·

In the evenings Donal and I sat by the peat fire. He did not say much in the evenings. He smoked a small clay pipe of tobacco he had been growing himself, in a green-house at the back, and the smoke of it was strong and a little sweet and the dog liked the smell. The dog had taken to lying with his back against Donal's boots in the evenings, which was a thing he had not done with any other man, and Donal accepted this without comment, the way a man accepts the choices of a creature whose judgment he has come to respect.

On the third evening Donal took down a small box from the dresser and opened it and took out a folded square of paper.

"My wife paints," he said. "She does not call them paintings. She calls them looks. A look at a thing on a particular morning. She does one or two a year. She sends them with me when I cross back. I keep them here. I do not put them on the wall. They were not made to be put on a wall. I keep them in this box. I take them out one at a time on certain evenings."

He laid the square of paper on the table between us and unfolded it. It was a small watercolour. It was a look, as he had said, at a thing. The thing was a stone on a beach. There was the stone in the foreground, dark grey, wet, with a small fringe of seaweed lying against its base. Behind the stone there was the shore, and beyond the shore there was the water, and beyond the water the dim blue line of the mainland mountains. The stone was the subject. The mountains were not. She had given the stone the attention. She had made the stone the kind of thing a man could look at for a long while and find new things in. The mountains were a small accident at the edge of the picture, a piece of distant scenery that had happened to be where it was.

I looked at it for a while. I looked at it for some long time, in fact. Donal smoked his pipe. The dog lay against his boots.

"It is the stone," I said at last.

"It is the stone," he said. "She has done many of the stones. She does the stones one by one. There are perhaps four hundred stones on the small bay where the cottage stands. She does not know how many are left. She does not count. She does one when one wants to be done. She has done, perhaps, sixty of them, in the years she has been there. She has perhaps three hundred and forty more. She will not finish. She does not mind. The work is the work. The finishing is a thing other people would have wanted. She does not."

I thought of the cataloguer in the long room of the library. I thought of his ledger, his small slips of paper. I thought of him saying, The work does not require an audience. It requires only attention and hands and time, and I have these things, and so I do it.

"There are more of them than I had thought," I said.

"More of who."

"The people who refused. The ones doing the small work."

"Yes," he said. "There are more than you thought. There are not many, but there are more. We are not so many as to fill a town. We are perhaps enough to populate a small island, if we lived together, but we do not live together. We live in our places, where our work has put us. We are a kind of country, although we have no country. We are a kind of family, although we are mostly strangers to each other. The list passes between us slowly. A name comes to me from somewhere, in a dream, in the way a name does. I write it down on a small piece of paper and I put the paper in this box. I have perhaps thirty names. The names mean very little. They are only the small accident of how the place announces who is in it. The work is the country, not the names."

He folded the watercolour gently and put it back in the box. He put the box back on the dresser. He took up his pipe again.

"You will meet her," he said, after a while. "When the loch allows. I will take you across. I will leave you there for as long as you need. I will not stay with you. I will tie up at the small jetty and I will sit on the boat and I will wait. You will go up to the cottage. You will do whatever you are going to do. I will not ask. I do not ask. When you are done, you will come down and I will take you back, or you will choose to stay, and I will come for you when you are ready, and if you are never ready, you will be ready in another way, and the loch will know."

"Donal."

"Yes."

"You speak as though you know what is on the island."

"I do not," he said. "I know what is on the part of it where my wife lives. I know nothing of the part where the cottage of your grandmother stands. I have not been there. The island is not so small as you might think. There is a hill in the middle and the cottages are at opposite ends. My wife has been to the cottage perhaps three times in forty years. She walks across the hill. She told me about it. She told me only what she could, which was not very much, and she made me promise to take whoever came, when one came, and not to ask, and not to interpret. I have kept the promise. I am keeping it now."

"Has anyone gone to the cottage."

"No," he said. "You will be the first. There has been one or two who came down this track. They went to my wife's end. They had been walking toward her. They had not known they were until they got here. The cottage end has waited, all this while, for someone walking toward it. I think you are the someone."

I did not speak. The peat fire made its small sound in the grate.

"You will see," he said. "You will see when you go up the path. You will know what is yours to know. Do not try to know it before then. The walking has earned you the knowing. Trust the walking."

· · ·

On the eighth day the wind dropped, and Donal stood at the door of the lean-to in the early morning and looked out at the loch for a long time without speaking. I came and stood beside him. The water was glass. The far hills were doubled in it. There were no gulls in the air. The boat at the jetty rode at her painter without movement.

"Today," he said.

"Yes."

"Pack what you need. Leave the rest with me. I will keep it. If you do not come back I will keep it for you anyway. It will be here. It is small enough. It is no trouble."

"All right."

I packed light. I took the green book with the curve folded between its pages. I took Iona's knife. I took a single change of clothes. I took bread Donal had baked the day before, and cheese, and two apples from his tree. I took the dog's bowl. I left almost everything else. I left the heavy things with him under the lean-to in a small corner against the wall, and he covered them with a square of canvas without comment.

I went to the boat. The dog went ahead of me. He had stood at the edge of the jetty several times in the eight days, looking out at the water in the way he had looked at the sea on the firth, considering it, and now he went down the steps and onto the boat without hesitation, and stood in the bow and looked forward as though he had been told, by some agency I had not been party to, that this was where he should be.

Donal cast off. He rowed us out from the jetty in long quiet pulls, the only sound the dipping of the oars in the still water. We went out across the bay toward the mouth of the loch. The dog stood in the bow with his ears forward. The sun came up at last over the eastern hills and laid a long band of gold across the water in front of us, and we rowed into the band of gold, and out beyond it, and the loch took us slowly outward toward the open mouth and the islands.

I did not look back at the bay. The bay had been good to me. I would have looked back later, perhaps, if there were a later. I looked forward, with the dog, at the water, at the islands, at the small green shape that began, as the sun came higher, to lift out of the haze in the distance to the west, the long low island with the small white cottage on it, and the deer perhaps coming down at this very hour to the shore to drink.

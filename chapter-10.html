Northbound

Chapter Ten

Eden

I came to the river Eden on a morning when there was mist along the water and the sun was somewhere above the mist trying to find a way down through it. The road I had been walking dropped into a wooded valley and the trees were old, oaks and ashes and a great many alders along the banks, and the air under them was cool and damp and smelled of leaf-mould and the river. The dog had grown excited as we came down. He had a particular excitement about water that I did not have. I had to call him back twice from the river path before I caught up.

The bridge across the Eden had been a road bridge, two lanes of grey tarmac with low parapets of red sandstone, and it crossed the river at a high point above a long pool. I went out to the middle of it and stood and looked down. The water was clear and dark and moved with that slow muscular slide that big rivers have when they are not in spate. There were trout in the pool. I could see them holding their places in the current as though they had been pinned there, occasionally drifting a little and finning back, and once a larger one rose and took something off the surface with a small clean dimple of sound.

I had known, when the bee woman said mind the river crossings, that this would be one of them. The Eden was a river I had crossed many times in my old life, on trains, going north for some reason or other I no longer cared to remember, and I had never once looked at it. I had read the word on a sign and the word had registered nothing. Eden. I leaned on the parapet now and I considered the word, and the river beneath it, and I felt for a moment a small sharpness that was something like shame. I had been given many small inheritances of beauty in the country I had grown up in, and I had taken almost none of them, and the country had carried on offering them anyway. The Eden had run under the train I had not looked out of for nineteen years.

The dog was already on the far side of the bridge, watching me. I went on across.

· · ·

The road on the far side ran for half a mile along the river before it climbed away, and the railway ran beside the road, and the railway crossed the river by its own bridge a little downstream. We walked along the road and the river was on our left and the railway on our right, and after a while I saw, on the railway, a train.

It was a passenger train. Four carriages. It had stopped at no station, on an open stretch of track, on a slight curve where the line ran between the embankment and a hawthorn hedge. It was the colour of the trains I had ridden on in the old life, white with a coloured stripe, although the colour of the stripe had faded in the seasons since it had stopped there. The doors of the train were open. All of them. Down the side of each carriage there were two doors, and all of them had been opened, and they had not been closed again, and the open doors made the train look in the long quiet morning like something with its mouths agape, as though it had been about to say a thing and had forgotten the thing.

I went up the embankment to it. I do not know why. I had been in trains a thousand times. I was not curious about the inside of a train. But the open doors had a quality of invitation, and I went up.

The carriages were empty.

I had not known what I had expected. I had not, I think, expected anything. There were no bags on the racks. There were no bodies in the seats. There was a single newspaper on the floor beneath one of the seats, gone soft with damp, and on a folding table that had been left down a paper coffee cup with the lid still on, and inside the coffee cup, when I lifted it, the tea or coffee that had been in it had dried to a black ring at the bottom. There was a small child's toy on the floor at the far end of one of the carriages. A blue plastic horse. I did not pick it up.

I went the length of the train through the carriages, the doors between them sliding open with their old soft hiss as I came to them, the dog at my heel. He had come up onto the train without any of his usual hesitations. He walked with me through the carriages and looked into each one and went on. We came at last to the front carriage and to the door of the driver's cab.

The cab door was open. I looked in. The driver's seat was empty. The brake handle was set, the small lever pulled all the way back into its rest position, the way a driver sets a brake when he is bringing the train to a stop on purpose, and not when he has stopped because he has been told to. The driver had stopped the train deliberately. He had set the brake. He had stood up from his seat. He had gone through the carriages, perhaps, and opened all the doors. Or he had opened only his own, and the passengers had opened theirs themselves. He had stepped down onto the embankment.

And then they had all gone, the driver and the passengers, into the country alongside the line, and the train had stayed where they had left it, with its doors agape, and the seasons had come and gone over it, and the doors had not been closed.

I stood in the cab and looked at the brake. The dog stood at the door of the cab and looked at me.

"All right," I said quietly.

I went back through the train. I closed the doors as I went. I do not know why I did this. It was a foolish work. It would matter to no one, the doors of a train standing on a forgotten line in a country that had finished with trains. But I closed them. I closed them carefully one by one. The doors slid on their tracks with the same small whisper they had always made, and each one settled into its frame with a soft thud, and I went on to the next, and the dog went with me. When I had closed the last door I stepped down from the train and stood in the long grass beside the embankment and looked along the carriages. The train looked, with its mouths shut at last, like a thing at rest. It was a small piece of order I had been able to give it. It was perhaps the only piece of order it would have.

I went down the embankment to the road.

· · ·

That night I camped on the far bank of the Eden a few miles upstream from the road bridge, in a sandy bay where the river had cut into the meadow and left a small flat place above the water. I made a fire of driftwood. I cooked the last of the cataloguer's bread with a spoon of the bee woman's honey and a pinch of the priest's salt, and the dog and I ate it sitting on the sand looking at the river going past. The mist had come back as the sun went down. It rose off the water in slow drifts and moved away over the meadow. The trout were rising again in the pool below us. I watched them rise and rise.

I thought of the train. I thought of the driver, who had brought it to a stop on a slight curve in an open stretch and had set the brake all the way back into its rest. I thought of the passengers who had opened their doors. I thought of the small blue plastic horse on the floor of the carriage. I thought of the bee woman saying he went out one morning into the garden and looked a long time at the hives and then came in and kissed me on the top of the head. I thought of Edmund saying it came in below the hearing. I thought of the curve in the children's drawings, soft hook at the end.

I thought: the driver heard it. I thought: the driver heard it and he was a careful man, he did not want to leave a thing dangerous, he did not want a great steel weight to keep going by itself across the country with nobody to mind it. He took the time to set the brake. He thought of the train as a creature that needed laying down. Then he stepped down onto the embankment and he went out into the country with his passengers, and they had all of them, perhaps, gone the same way, into the same field, and lain in the grass, and listened.

I had been afraid to think this thought. I had been carrying it along the road, with the curve folded into the green book in the pack on my back, and I had not let myself think it out in words. Now I had thought it. The river ran on past me. The trout went on rising. The dog sighed beside me on the sand and laid his head on his paws and looked at me sideways with his slow soft eye, and the mist came down over the meadow.

I did not know what to do with the thought now that I had let it out. I did not know whether to be sad for the people in the field, or glad for them. I did not know, finally, whether they had been right or wrong to step down from the train. There were two ways of looking at it. Edmund had said the stone had gone out of his shoe. The bee woman had said someone had to do for the bees in winter. The curve hung in the air between them and I could not yet draw a line through it.

The fire burned low. The dog slept. I lay back on the sand and looked at the stars coming out one by one above the river mist and thought, before I slept, that I had been given to know one thing today, although the meaning of it was not yet clear. I had been given to know that the thing that had come had been heard by careful men and trusted children and old women in their gardens, and they had each of them answered as their own care had asked of them, and each answer had been honest. There was no shame in any of it. There were only the careful and the trusted and the old, and the things they loved, and what those things had asked of them in the moment. I rolled onto my side. The river spoke quietly to its bank. The dog's breath went in and out. I slept under the high cold stars and dreamed of a small blue horse on the floor of an empty carriage, going nowhere.

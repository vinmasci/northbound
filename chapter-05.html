Northbound

Chapter Five

The Bee Woman

I came down into Hawes on a Tuesday, although the day of the week was a thing I had to work out by the angle of the sun and even then I do not believe I had it right. The town stood in its grey solid way along its main street with the fells coming down green above it on both sides, and the river running fast and brown between two of its bridges, and the rooks turning slowly in the air over the church tower as they had always done. There was a hand-painted sign nailed to a fence post at the bottom of the road that said, in white letters on green, HONEY. ASK AT THE LAST HOUSE.

I stood and looked at the sign. The paint was not new. It was not very old either. A person had painted it within the last year or two, and a person had hammered it to the post, and a person was perhaps still selling honey, although I could not imagine what they would take for it.

The dog was very interested in the sign. He sniffed at the post and sniffed at the bottom of the sign and looked up at me. I took the sign as an instruction and we went on into the town.

The street was empty. The shops were as they had been left, which is to say in different states, depending on who had been minding them when the leaving had happened. The greengrocer was bare and scrubbed and the door propped open with a milk crate. The pub was locked. The bookshop had its door wedged with a doorstop and inside the books stood on their shelves in their old patient rows, and I went in and stood there for a few minutes, smelling the smell of paper and old wood, and the dog stood at the threshold and looked at me as though to say it was not the kind of place he understood. I came out.

We walked the length of the street and out the other end, and there at the very last, set back from the road behind a low wall of mossed stone, was a long low house of grey rough-cast with a slate roof and a yellow door. There were three hives in the garden. There was a column of bees moving between the hives and the lavender that ran along the wall, and the column was the first proper movement of small creatures I had seen in some long time, and I stood in the road and watched it for a while before I went up to the door.

· · ·

The woman who opened the door was perhaps sixty, perhaps seventy, with a face the weather had been at for many years. She was dressed in a man's shirt and a long apron of waxed cotton and her grey hair was tied back with a strip of green cloth. She looked at me without surprise. She looked at the dog without surprise. The dog sat down on the step.

"You'll be wanting honey," she said.

"I read the sign," I said.

"Aye," she said. She did not move from the doorway. She looked past me into the road as though to see whether anyone else were coming. "How many?"

"How many what?"

"How many of you," she said.

"Just the two of us," I said.

She nodded, as though this were the answer she had expected. She stood aside and made a small gesture with her hand, and we went in.

· · ·

The kitchen at the back of the house was warm. There was a range going, the small kind that burns wood and not much, and the kettle stood on its plate with a low whisper of steam. There was a long pine table scrubbed pale, and on the table jars of honey in two ranks, perhaps thirty jars in all, the honey in them ranging from the pale gold of the year's first flow to a deep amber that was almost brown. There were three loaves of bread on a board under a clean cloth. There was a pat of yellow butter on a plate. The smell in the kitchen was of bread and beeswax and woodsmoke and the faintest under-note of something herbal and sharp.

"Sit," she said. I sat.

She did not ask me my name. I did not ask her hers. She filled the kettle from a jug and set it back on the range and took down two cups from a dresser. She cut two slices of bread and laid them on plates and spread them with butter and lifted the lid from one of the jars of honey and ladled honey onto each, and slid one plate across the table to me, and one to herself. She sat. She looked at me.

"Eat," she said.

I ate. I had not tasted honey in I do not know how long. The bread was that morning's bread, still soft inside its crust. The butter was salt and yellow and the honey was gold and the three of them together did a thing in my mouth that I do not have words for. I ate slowly. I did not want to be done eating.

The dog had lain down by the range. He had been given a bowl of something I had not seen her prepare. He was eating it without urgency.

"They keep their work," she said at length. She was looking out of the window at the column of bees in the lavender. "That's what I find. Some things kept their work and some things didn't. The bees kept it. The dogs kept it. The bread keeps it, if you keep at the bread. The grass kept it. The stone walls kept it for a season and then started to come down."

I did not answer. I understood that she did not require an answer. I ate the bread and the honey.

"My husband didn't keep it," she said. She said this in the same tone, as one item in a list. "He went out one morning into the garden and stood there a long time looking at the hives, and then he came in and kissed me on the top of the head, and went back out and walked off down the road. I watched him from the window. He went down to the bridge and then he turned left along the river path and I did not see him again." She paused. "He had been a good husband. I did not begrudge him going."

I set down the bread.

"You didn't go," I said.

"No."

"Why not."

She looked at me with mild eyes. "The bees," she said. "Someone's got to do for them in winter. They are not so much trouble. But you cannot leave them to it altogether or they go down."

I waited. She did not say anything more. I understood that this was the whole of her answer.

· · ·

I stayed two nights with the bee woman. I do not know why she allowed it. I do not know why I asked. The first night I slept on a settle in the kitchen with a wool blanket over me and the dog at my feet, and in the morning she set me to splitting wood by the back door, which I did badly at first and better as the morning went on. She watched me from the kitchen window for a while and then went about her business and did not watch me again. She made the bread that day in front of me, kneading it on the table with her sleeves rolled up to the elbow, her forearms strong and brown, and she did not speak while she kneaded. Bread, I came to understand, was a thing she did with her whole attention and not in conversation.

In the afternoon she opened one of the hives. She gave me a veil of net and white gloves and we went out together, and she took the lid off the top box and lifted out a frame heavy with comb and she held it up to the sun. The bees crawled over her hands and she did not flinch. They crawled over my hands and I did not flinch either, although my heart was loud in my chest. She showed me the queen, a long pale-amber body in the moving crowd. She showed me brood, capped and uncapped, the small white grubs in their cells. She showed me the dance of a returning forager and read the dance for me, the angle of the sun, the distance to the flower. She did all this without ceremony. It was as though she were showing me how a familiar tool worked, or where the cups were kept.

"They go out and come back," she said. "That is the whole of it. They go out into the world and they come back to the hive. They do not ask whether the hive should be a hive. They do not ask whether the world should be a world. They go out and come back. There is a great deal to be said for it."

"Yes," I said.

She looked at me sideways. She slid the frame back into the box. She closed the hive.

"You're walking," she said. It was not a question.

"North," I said.

"Aye," she said. "I thought as much."

"You don't ask me where."

"You'll know it when you get there," she said. "Or you'll go past it and come back. People who are walking know where they are walking to or they are not really walking, they are only running away. You are walking. I can see you are walking."

I said nothing.

"Take a jar," she said, when we were back in the kitchen. "Take two. They keep. Do not leave them in the sun. Eat them when you are tired. There is iron in the dark ones."

"What can I give you," I said.

She considered this for a moment. Then she said, "Split a stack of wood for me before you go. Enough for a fortnight. That is what you can give me."

"Yes," I said.

· · ·

I split wood for her on the morning I left until the muscles in my shoulders shook. I stacked it under the eave at the back, against the wall, the way she showed me, with the bark side out so the rain would shed. She brought me water in a jug and a heel of bread and watched me work and went away and came back. When the stack was as high as my hip she came out and put her hand on my arm and said, "Enough." We went into the kitchen and I drank the water and ate the bread.

At the door she gave me the two jars wrapped in a square of cloth, and a third loaf, and a small twist of paper that turned out, when I looked at it later, to contain a few pinches of dried thyme. She crouched and put her hand on the dog's head. The dog received it with grave attention.

"He is a good one," she said.

"I know it," I said.

She stood up. She looked at me, and then past me into the road.

"The thing that came," she said. "It came in below the hearing. People will tell you different but that is what I heard. It came in below the hearing and it asked, and most people answered, and that was the end of it for them, and a kind of beginning, perhaps, although I will not say. I did not answer. I had work in front of me. The bees were in their second flow. There was no time to be answering anything. Whether I will answer when it comes again I cannot say. It comes and goes. It is not in a hurry."

I stood in the doorway with the bread under my arm. I did not speak. The dog had gone out into the road and was waiting.

"Go on, then," she said, gently. "It is a long way to where you are going. Mind the river crossings. Mind the high places after dark."

I went out. I turned at the gate to look back. She was already shutting the door. I heard the latch fall. I walked off down the road with the dog at my heel and the bees in their lavender behind me, and I did not look back again.

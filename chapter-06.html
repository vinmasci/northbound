Northbound

Chapter Six

The Cataloguer

The library was in a market town I will not name because it is a town that I might want to keep to myself, the way the bee woman kept her bees. The library stood between a bank with its windows boarded and a chemist with its windows broken. It was a Carnegie building of red brick with stone facings, and the words FREE LIBRARY were cut into the lintel above the door in a hand from another century, and the door was open. There was no card on it. There was only the door open as it had been open in the days when libraries were things one walked into without thinking.

I went up the steps. The dog stayed in the road. He had developed views about which buildings he would enter and which he would not, and I had stopped trying to argue with them.

Inside, the air was cool and dim and smelled of old paper and floor polish. There was a desk to the left of the door and on the desk a small brass bell of the kind one rang to summon attention, although the desk was empty and there was nothing to be summoned to. Beyond the desk the long room of the library opened out, with its tall shelves and its high windows and its galleries running round at the upper level, and the books stood in their thousands in their old wooden ranks.

I stood for a while in the entrance and let the building settle round me. I had not been in a library in many years. It came back to me in a rush of sense-memory I had not known I was carrying, the smell of it, the muffled quality of the sound, the way the dust hung in the light from the high windows. I took a step forward and the floorboard creaked beneath me, and a man somewhere among the shelves said, without raising his voice, "I am here. Come through."

I went through.

· · ·

He was at the back of the long room, at a table he had pulled into a clear space between the stacks, and he was sitting at the table with a book open in front of him and a thin pencil in his hand. He was perhaps fifty, with thinning sand-coloured hair and steel spectacles, and he wore a grey jumper over a shirt and a dark tie, and the tie was knotted with care, although the cuffs of the shirt were frayed. There were three stacks of books on the table. There was an open ledger.

He looked up at me over his spectacles and nodded. He did not seem surprised. I had stopped being surprised at people not being surprised.

"I'm passing through," I said.

"I know," he said. "Most people who come in are passing through. Sit if you'd like. I've some tea."

There was a small camping stove on a side table. There was a tin kettle. There were two cups. He had been expecting nothing and he had been ready for whatever came.

I sat. He made tea. We drank it. It was thin and very hot and I drank it gratefully. He did not ask me where I had come from or where I was going. He asked me whether the road from Hawes was passable, and whether the bridge at Aysgarth was still standing. I said yes to both, although I had not come by Aysgarth and could not have known. He thanked me as though I had told him something useful.

He pushed the ledger toward me when I had finished my tea.

"I am cataloguing," he said. "You may want to see how I am cataloguing. People sometimes do."

I took the ledger. I opened it. The pages were ruled in two columns and written in his small, very neat hand. The left-hand column held what I took to be titles, and the right-hand column held single words, or short phrases, that I did not at first understand. The Mill on the Floss — rivers, sisters. Northanger Abbey — rooms and rooms. Mrs Dalloway — the bell, the day. King Lear — weather, daughters, end. Bleak House — fog, fog, fog.

I read down a page. I read down another. I went back to the first page and read it again. I looked up at him.

"It is not Dewey," I said.

"No," he said.

"It is not by author."

"No."

"What is it?"

He took the ledger gently from my hand and turned it back to face him. He looked down at his page and tapped it once with the pencil.

"It is by what they are about," he said. "What they are really about. The single thing the book is doing under everything else it is doing. I have been reading them for that and writing it down, and then I am putting them on the shelves in groups. So that a person who comes in, if a person comes in, can find what they need without having known what they needed when they came in."

"Who is going to come in?"

He looked at me. He looked at me over the spectacles in a way I will remember.

"You came in," he said.

I did not have an answer to that.

· · ·

He showed me the shelves. He had not yet got far. He had perhaps two hundred books re-shelved in his new way. They were arranged in groups of four and five, each group with a small slip of paper tucked into the front of one of the books, on which he had written a word in his small careful hand. Rivers. Mothers. Endings of empires. Weather and grief. Houses one cannot leave. Houses one cannot return to. The kindness of strangers. The unkindness of one's own. Light through high windows. The slips were small and elegant, the script the same as in the ledger.

I walked along the shelves and read his slips. The dog had come in at last, I do not know when, and was lying in a patch of sun by the door with his head on his paws watching me. I came to a shelf with a slip that read Walking, alone or accompanied, and I stopped in front of it. There were perhaps eight books in the group. The Old Ways. The Wild Places. The Living Mountain. The Pilgrim's Progress. As I Walked Out One Midsummer Morning. A small green-bound copy of The Odyssey. A book I did not know with a plain blue cover. Cold Mountain.

"You see," he said, "if you came in not knowing what you needed."

"Yes."

"I would walk you to that shelf."

"Yes."

"Take one," he said. "Take one if you want. They're not mine. They're not anyone's. They were the town's, and I am tending them while no one is using them, and someone may come who can use one of them, and I would like that person to be you."

I stood in front of the shelf for a long time. I took down The Living Mountain and held it. I had never read it. I had heard of it. The cover was soft and the pages were thumbed at the edges. Someone had read it many times before I had come to take it. I put it in the side pocket of my pack.

"Thank you," I said.

"Don't thank me," he said. "Thank the woman who read it before you. She wore the corners down. You are taking the warmth she put into it."

· · ·

We sat for a while at his table afterwards. He made another pot of tea. The light from the high windows had moved by then onto the spines of the upper shelves and made them glow in their old golds and dim reds. I asked him at last what I had been wanting to ask since I had sat down.

"Why this," I said. "Why this work. With no one to come in. With perhaps no one ever to come in."

He set down the teapot. He looked at me, mild, considering.

"You want me to say a noble thing," he said. "I will not, because I do not have one. I will tell you instead what is true. I worked here for thirty-one years. I did not love it always. I loved it sometimes. When the thing came, when the people went, I went out into the street with everyone else, and I stood in the street and I looked at the sky and I felt what was in it, and I thought: very well. I went home. I sat in my chair. I had a small drink. I waited."

"For what."

"To know what to do." He folded his hands. "I had thought I would go, like the others. I had thought I would walk down to the river and lie down in the grass and let it have me. But when I sat in my chair I did not feel that I wanted to. I felt that I wanted to come here in the morning and put the books in better order. It was a small wanting. It was not the great wanting that the others had. It was small enough that it did not match what was in the air. So I did not go. I came here in the morning. And I have been coming here every morning since."

"What if no one comes."

"Then I will have spent my time well anyway." He reached for the teapot again. "It is the work that mattered to me. Not the readers. I had thought, all those years, that it was the readers, but it was the work. The readers were a great gift. I see now that the work was the deeper one. I was given the work and I am doing it. The work does not require an audience. It requires only attention and hands and time, and I have these things, and so I do it. There is nothing else I would rather do. There never was."

He poured. The tea was very pale this time, the leaves spent. I drank it.

"What word would you write for me," I said.

He looked at me carefully. He looked at me for what felt like a long time.

"I would not write only one for you," he said at last. "I would write three. Rivers. Returning. The thing not yet seen. Those would be your three."

I did not know what to say to that.

"Go on," he said gently. "Your dog wants to be off."

The dog was at the door, looking back at us.

I rose. I shook his hand. His hand was thin and dry and warm. I went out down the long room past the shelves, with the small green book in my pack, and at the door I turned and looked back at him sitting at his table in the patch of late light. He had already opened his ledger and bent his head to it. The pencil moved in his hand.

I went down the steps and into the road and the dog walked at my heel, and I thought, as I walked away, that if I had been asked at the door of the building of glass, on any of the nineteen years of my mornings there, what the thing was I had really been doing under the work I had been doing, I would not have been able to give an answer. I would not have known. The building of glass had not asked me. The building of glass had not cared. I was not the building of glass's work. I was the building of glass's audience, and there had been a great many of us, and we had all of us been small.

I walked through the town and out of it, and when the road climbed I climbed with it, and I thought of the man bent over his ledger in the long room with the dust hanging in the light, doing his small wanting, his work that no one would come for. Rivers, he had written for me. Returning. The thing not yet seen. I rolled the words over in my mouth. I did not know yet which of them would prove most true. I had a feeling that all three would, in their own time, and that the order of them mattered, although I could not yet say how.

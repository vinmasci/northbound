Northbound

Chapter Eleven

The Forest

I crossed into Scotland on a morning when there was no announcement of it. There was no border that one could see. There was a road, and the road went on, and at a certain point the bracken on the verges had begun to look a little different, and the stone of the walls had taken a different colour, and a thrush singing in a sycamore had perhaps a slightly different inflection in its singing, although that was likely my own ear inventing what it had wished to find. I had imagined I would feel a thing at the border. I felt only the road continuing under my feet.

I had been walking for what I now think must have been a hundred and twenty days. I had lost count for a long time, which is a freedom, and then I had recovered it for a little, which is a vanity, and then I had lost it again, which is a kindness one does for oneself.

The road climbed for two days into the high country of Galloway. It was a quieter country than the dales had been, less worked, more left to itself. There were lochs in long slow valleys, and farms set back behind their fields, and on the third afternoon I came to the edge of the forest.

· · ·

It was a working forest, or had been. I knew this because the trees had been planted in rows. They were sitka spruce, in a great mass, their lower branches dead, their tops climbing into a single dim canopy, the floor between them brown and littered with needles and very still. Such forests are not really forests. They are crops standing very tall. The dog and I went in along a forestry track and the canopy closed above us and the light came down green and was not much.

I had thought the country quiet before I came into the forest. I had been wrong. There had been a great deal of small sound in the open country and I had not realised how much. There had been wind in grass, and birds at small business in the hedges, and the running of water, and the cropping of sheep, and the faint friction of one's own clothing as one walked. The forest had none of these things. The trees were too close-set for wind to come through. There were no birds. There was no water that I could hear. The needles on the floor were too soft to register a footstep. The dog's pads made no sound. I made some sound, but only because I was carrying things in a pack, and they shifted as I moved, and once or twice I cleared my throat for the comfort of it.

It was the dog who showed me first. He was walking ahead of me, as he did, and at a certain point he stopped and put his ears up and stood. He held very still. I came up to him. He did not look at me. He was looking down the track ahead of us, and after a moment he turned and looked back the way we had come. There was nothing on the track. There had been nothing on the track for an hour. He looked at the wall of trees on his left. He looked at the wall of trees on his right. His ears moved slightly. He shook himself with a small private shudder, and walked on.

I walked on with him. I tried to listen in the way he had been listening. I listened for sound, which is the natural thing. There was no sound. I listened for the absence of sound, which is a more difficult work and which the bee woman had perhaps been describing when she had said it came in below the hearing. I listened for the shape of the absence, the way an absence has a shape if you give it long enough. After a while, walking very slowly between the dim trees, with the dog at my heel, I began to feel that there was such a shape.

It is hard to describe. I have not had to describe it before. It was like the feeling one has in a high room when an old machine, that one has not been aware of, switches off. The room afterwards is the same room. It is not louder. It is not quieter, since the machine had been below one's notice. But the room is changed, and one knows it is changed, and one cannot at first say why. The forest had this quality. There was a thing that had gone out of it, and the thing had perhaps gone out of it some time ago, and the forest had been holding the shape of the thing's leaving ever since.

I walked through this shape. I did not know what to do with it. The dog walked with me. He had stopped putting his ears up. He had perhaps decided that the shape was something he could not act on and so was not worth acting on, in the way of dogs, who do not waste themselves on what they cannot fight or chase or eat. I admired him for it. I had not yet learned the trick.

· · ·

An hour into the forest the track came to a junction. There was a small clearing at the junction, perhaps the size of a tennis court, where the trees had been cut and the stumps left. In the middle of the clearing someone had made a cairn. It was a careful cairn, a yard high, of fist-sized stones laid in courses. There was no inscription. There was no cross. There was nothing on the cairn but the stones, set one upon another, on a mat of needles in the middle of the cleared place.

I went to it. The dog stayed at the edge of the clearing.

I walked round the cairn slowly. The stones had been gathered from somewhere, perhaps from a beck nearby. They were rounded, water-shaped. I did not touch the cairn. I had a strong feeling that it was not for touching. I stood at it and looked at it for a long time.

I thought: someone came in here, and stood in this place, and decided that a marker should be made here, although there was no body to mark and nothing in particular to remember. They went and got the stones, perhaps over more than one day, and they brought them here in a pack or in a barrow, and they set them one on another in the slow patient work of cairn-building. They did not write on them. They did not need to. The cairn was the writing. It said here, and it said only here.

I thought about whether to add a stone. It is what one does at a cairn one comes upon. But I had brought no stone with me, and I did not feel that I had earned the right to take one from the ground here for the purpose. The cairn had been made by someone who knew the place. I was passing through. I stood in front of it for a long while with my head a little bowed and the dog watching me from the edge of the clearing, and at last I turned and walked on.

The dog, when I came to him, did a thing he had not done before. He pressed his head against the back of my knee, hard, and stood there a moment, and then walked on with me up the track. I do not know what he had understood. I think he had understood that I had needed it, and he had given it. He has a great economy with such things. He gives what is needed and no more.

· · ·

We came out of the forest two hours later onto a high open hill. The sun was on the hill and the wind was on the hill and the world had its noise back, the wind in the long grass, and a single buzzard mewing somewhere above us, and very far below a beck running in a stony bed. The dog ran out into the open and turned circles in the grass and barked once for the simple pleasure of the sound, which was a thing he had not done before. I stood at the edge of the trees and looked back into them. The trees stood in their dim ranks. The shape was still in there. It had not come out with us.

I had begun, in the days since the train at the river, to allow myself a thought I had been keeping at arm's length. The thought was this: there had been more than one thing. The signal, or the note, or the descending curve, or whatever it was that had come, had not perhaps come once and gone away. It had come, and gone, and come again, and stayed in some places longer than in others. The forest had held it for some long time. The kitchen with the apple had held it less long. The bee woman's lavender had not held it at all, because the bees had gone on doing their work, and the work was a kind of flushing of the air. The library and the rectory had held it lightly because the cataloguer and the priest had each made their own small noise against it, the noise of attention given without distraction, which is perhaps the only noise the thing cannot pass through.

If this was right, the road north was carrying me through a country still full of unequal weather. There would be places where the thing was thick. There would be places where it was thin. There would be places where it was gone. I would know, perhaps, when I was in one or another. The dog would know first, and would tell me by his ears. I would learn to walk by his ears.

I turned away from the trees. I went out onto the hill. The wind had a smell in it of bracken and bog and something like distant sea, although the sea was still many miles off. The dog ran ahead. The track ran down across the open ground toward a farmhouse in the valley with smoke coming out of its chimney, a thin blue smoke, going straight up.

I had not seen smoke from a chimney since Edmund's. I stopped on the track and looked at it.

"Well," I said to the dog, who had come back to me. "Someone is at home."

He looked at the farmhouse. He looked at me. He looked at the farmhouse again. He did not seem worried. I took it as encouragement, and we went on down toward the smoke.

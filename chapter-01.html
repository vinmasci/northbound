Northbound

Chapter One

Motorway

The motorway had become a long pale river of weeds. I walked the central reservation where the going was easiest, the dog a little ahead, sometimes stopping to look back at me as if to check I was still the same person I had been a mile ago.

There was no wind. There had not been wind for some days. The sky over the Pennines held that washed grey of late August, the colour of an old shirt, and the hills beyond Shap were beginning to brown at their edges. Somewhere a crow spoke once and then thought better of it.

I had stopped counting the days. At first I had kept a small book and made marks in it the way prisoners are said to do, but the marks had begun to feel like a kind of pleading, and one evening near Lancaster I had taken the book out of my pack and laid it on a low wall and walked on without it. I do not know why. The dog had looked at me for a long moment and then trotted after.

We slept that night in the lee of an overturned lorry. I made a small fire of split pallet wood and we ate together, the dog and I, from the same tin. He had taken to pressing the side of his head against my knee when he ate, as though the act of eating were something that ought to be witnessed. I sat in the firelight and watched him and thought: this is the world now. A dog leaning against a knee. A tin scraped clean. The slow blue dusk coming down over the fells.

I had been walking north for what I believed was the better part of a season. I was going to a house I half remembered, on a road I half remembered, above a sea-loch whose name I had perhaps never properly known. My grandmother had lived there once. The kitchen had smelled of peat and wet wool. There had been a window that gave out onto water, and beyond the water a low green island with one white cottage on it, and I had been told as a child that on certain mornings you could see deer come down to drink. I did not know if any of this was still true. I did not know if it had been true then. But I was walking toward it anyway, because a person must walk toward something, and the things I had once walked toward were all of them gone.

· · ·

The dog had come to me in the spring, near a roundabout south of Wigan. He had been sitting in the middle of the road with the patience of an old man at a bus stop, his coat the colour of wet bracken, and when I had stopped he had risen and walked the last yards to me and put his head against my thigh and stayed there. There had been no need for words, which was good, because I had not used many in some time. I had cut a length of rope for a lead and then taken it off again the next morning when I saw that he would not leave me.

I called him nothing. He answered to the small movements of my hand. He answered to the click of my tongue. I had decided early that I would not name him, because to name a thing was to make a hostage of it against the world, and the world was no longer a place that gave hostages back. He seemed to understand this and to accept it, and we walked on together, nameless and unweighted, north into the long quiet country.

What he ate I cannot fully say. I shared what I had. He found things in the verges I did not look at too closely. Once I watched him take a hare from cover with a single low rush, and bring it back and lay it at my feet, and look up at me with a politeness that asked nothing. We had eaten well that night. He did this again perhaps once in a fortnight, never more. He understood the size of what was needed and did not exceed it.

I had known many people in my old life and I had not understood any of them as well as I understood this dog.

· · ·

The motorway in the morning was full of birds. They had taken the whole carriageway as a kind of open ground and they rose in a slow grey curtain when we came on and settled again behind us as though we had passed through a single long bird and out the other side. There were starlings and thrushes and the occasional crow. Once a kestrel held itself fixed above a junction sign and we stopped and watched it together, the dog and I, until it stooped and was gone.

I had used to drive this road. I remembered service stations whose names I could no longer call up, and the particular smell of warm tarmac, and a kind of low-grade ache behind the eyes that I had not understood at the time was a form of grief. I had driven north many times for many reasons and had never once stopped for what I now stopped for. The way the wind moved over a field of unharvested barley. The way a thrush stood on a fence post and considered me. The fact that there was time, and time, and time.

I came to a junction near Tebay where the slip road curved down into a place that had once been a service station. There were the long low buildings, the petrol islands with their hoods raised like the mouths of feeding birds, and beyond them a paddock that ran down to a beck. The doors of the building had been propped open with a stone. I went inside.

The shelves had been picked over but not stripped. Whoever had come through had taken what they wanted and left the rest in something like order, which I had come to recognise as a kind of signature. The work of a person not in fear. I took a tin of peaches and a packet of porridge oats and a bar of chocolate so old it had gone pale and chalky on its surface. I left a tin of tomatoes I did not need. I do not know why. It is a thing I had begun to do.

In the corner of the shop a small radio was sitting on a stool. It was a bright orange thing with a silver dial. I had passed perhaps fifty radios in fifty places and had stopped trying them long ago. But this one had a card propped against it, handwritten, in a careful adult hand: Still works. Try it.

I looked at the card a long time. The dog had come in behind me and was sitting in the doorway with his back to the light, watching the road. I picked up the radio. There was a small switch on the side. I turned it.

For a moment there was nothing. Then a hiss, and beneath the hiss something I could not at first identify, a kind of low pressure on the ear, a feeling more than a sound, the way you feel a lorry in your chest before you hear it. The dog turned his head sharply and looked at me.

I switched it off.

I stood there a long time in the dim shop with the radio in my hand. Then I put it back on the stool and turned the card over and laid it face down. I went out into the light and the dog rose and we walked on.

· · ·

That night I camped in the lee of a hawthorn at the edge of a field. I did not make a fire. I lay on my back in the long grass and watched the stars come out one by one in their old order, and I thought of the radio, and of the hand that had written the card, and of the way the dog had turned his head. I thought of my grandmother's kitchen. I thought of the low green island.

I had been walking for a long time and I had begun to understand that the walking was no longer entirely toward something. It was also, in some part I could not name, an answer. The world had asked me a question I had not heard, and the only reply I knew how to make was to put one foot in front of the other and keep going north. Whether I would arrive at any answer worth the question I did not know. The dog lay against my side in the grass. His ribs rose and fell. The night was quiet in the particular way the nights had become quiet, which was not like the old quiet at all.

I did not sleep for a long while. When I did, I dreamed of water.

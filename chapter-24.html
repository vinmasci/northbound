Northbound

Chapter Twenty-Four

The Letters

I slept the first night in the small upstairs room with the dog at my feet, and I woke before light to the sound of the wind in the gable, and I lay a long while listening to it before I rose. I went down the stair in the half-dark. I built up the fire. I made tea from the leaves I had brought with me, in the pot with the knitted cosy. I sat at the table and drank it slowly while the light came in at the front window.

I did not, in those first days, open the drawers in the upstairs room. I did not go into the small shed at the side. I did not walk south across the hill to the other end of the island, where Donal's wife was, although I knew she was there. I did not look at the cottage as a thing with hidden rooms or hidden answers. I lived in it, in its surface, the way a person lives in any house when they first take it on. I ate in it. I slept in it. I lit the fire in the morning. I went out in the afternoons and walked the headlands above the bay with the dog, and we watched the seals on the rocks, and once we saw the deer coming down to drink at dusk, and I sat on a stone above the small inlet and watched them, and the dog sat beside me and watched them too, and we did not move until they had finished and gone.

I do not know how long I lived in the cottage like that before I opened the drawers. I think it was perhaps a week. It may have been longer. The days had the quality, by then, of days one lives without measuring. I had been measuring days for a long time, on the road, although I had stopped early on, and then started again, and then stopped. In the cottage I did not measure. The day began. The day was the day. The day ended. I lit a candle. I went up to bed.

· · ·

It was a wet morning when I opened them. The rain had come in from the west and was running grey down the windows, and the bay below the cottage was lost in the grey, and I had built the fire up and was sitting on the floor in front of it with the dog. I had taken down the small Bible from the chair upstairs and brought it down with me, and I had been reading a few pages at random, the way one reads a book one is not in any hurry to finish. After a while I closed it. I sat looking at the fire.

I rose at last. I went up the stairs.

The chest of drawers had three drawers. I opened the top one first. There was folded linen in it, sheets and pillowcases, very old, smelling faintly of lavender. There was a small muslin bag of dried lavender at the back, the cloth gone yellow with age. I closed the drawer.

I opened the middle drawer. There were a few items of clothing, a woman's, very plain. A grey wool jumper folded carefully. Two heavy shirts in dark wool. A wool skirt. A pair of thick stockings. A scarf, faded blue, hand-knitted. The clothes had a faint smell of the sea. I closed the drawer.

I opened the bottom drawer.

The bottom drawer was full of papers. They were in two stacks. The stack on the left was bound with a length of red cotton thread tied in a bow. The stack on the right was loose, and on top of it was a single sheet of paper, folded once, with a single word written on the outside, in pencil, in a hand I had last seen when I was seventeen years old.

The word was my name.

· · ·

I sat down on the floor in front of the chest of drawers with the folded paper in my hands. I sat for some long time. I did not open it at once. I think I was, in some part of myself, holding the moment as a person holds a small bird that has come down into their hand. I did not want the moment to fly off into knowing. I wanted to sit a little while longer in the small space where the paper was a paper with my name on it and not yet a paper that had said something to me. I had walked a hundred and seventy days for the paper. I could let it sit in my hand for half an hour more.

The dog came up the stairs after a while. He came over and sat on the boards beside me. He did not press himself against my leg. He had grown careful about my hours.

I unfolded the paper.

The hand was my grandmother's. It was a small slanting hand in pencil, gone faint in places where the pencil had been a soft one. The letter was not long. It was perhaps a page and a half. The date at the top was a date from twenty-six years before, when I would have been twenty-three, in my second year in the building of glass.

I read it. I read it a second time. I sat with it on the boards for a long time.

· · ·

I do not know if I should set the letter down here. It was a letter from a woman to her grandson. It was a private letter. I have thought about it a long while, since, on whether to write it down for you. I have decided I will write down some of it. I will not write down all of it. There are parts that belong to me and to her and to the cottage in which I read them. The rest I give you.

She began the letter, "My dear boy."

She wrote, in her quick small slanting hand:

If you are reading this, then you have come to the cottage. I do not know if you will come. I hope you will. I have left this letter for you because I want you to find something here when you arrive, even if I am not here to give it to you in person. I have a feeling I will not be. I have had this feeling for some time. The reasons for it are not important.

You will be wondering, I expect, what this place is, and why it has been kept, and by whom. The cottage is a small piece of the world that has been kept by a small number of people for some long while. It is not a special cottage, in itself. There are perhaps a dozen such places in this country and the country to the west. They are kept by the people who have learned to keep them. The keeping is passed. I am the keeper now. Someone gave it to me. I will give it to someone else when my time has come, or, if no one is ready, I will leave the cottage to itself, with this letter and a few others in the bottom drawer, and the place will keep itself for a while, and will wait for whoever comes.

Now I will tell you a thing that you may not believe. You will perhaps think me a foolish old woman with a head full of weather. That is your right. I will tell you anyway, because the writing of it for you is part of the keeping, and because I will not have many other chances.

The world speaks. It speaks always. It speaks the way water speaks in a stone basin, very small, very slow, very low. What it says is not great. What it says is: here is a stone, here is a wave, here is a creature beside you, here is your hand. It does not say great things. It does not need to. The small things are enough.

For most of the long time of human creatures, we lived in places where this voice could be heard. We lived where there was wind and there was water and there were creatures in the dark beyond our fires, and we made our small noise against the great quiet, and we heard the great quiet underneath. We knew it was there. We did not always know what it was saying. But we knew the shape of its saying.

In the last several lifetimes we have built a noise of a different kind. You know this noise. It is the noise of the world you live in. I do not need to describe it. It has filled every place. It has filled the air with engines, and the night with light, and the day with screens, and the silence with the soft hum of the next thing about to ask for our attention. The noise is so great that the voice of the world cannot be heard inside it. We did not, most of us, even know that there had been a voice. We had been born into the noise. We had thought the noise was the world.

I will tell you what I think will happen, my boy. I cannot tell you how I know. I am an old woman in a cottage on an island. I do not have facts to give you. I have only the feeling I have had for a long time, since I was your age, and which has been growing in me with every year. The feeling is this. The noise will not last. It is too great. It cannot bear its own greatness. It will, in some way, fail. I do not know how. Perhaps it will be slow. Perhaps it will be sudden. I do not think it will be a war or a sickness, although those things may come too. I think the noise will simply, one day, falter. And in the failing of it, the voice of the world will become audible to all who have ears, and what the voice will be saying, gently, to those who hear it for the first time after a lifetime in the noise, is what the voice has always said: you are tired, you are very tired, you have made yourself tired, you may lay it down.

Most people, hearing it, will lay it down. They will be glad to lay it down. They will not have known how much they were carrying until they were given permission to set it down. They will lay it down and they will go, in their own way, in their own places, into the world that was always under the noise.

A few will not. The few will be those who have already, in their own small way, been laying it down a little at a time, year after year, in their gardens and their kitchens and their workshops. They have not been waiting for the voice. They have been doing the small work of being small. They have made bread, kept bees, mended ropes, written words on slips of paper. They will be invited to lay it down, and they will say, gently, no thank you, I have my work to be getting on with. They will go on with the work. They will be the keepers of the small.

I would like you to be one of the keepers, my boy. I do not say this to put a burden on you. The keepers do not feel themselves burdened. They feel themselves fortunate. They have been allowed to do their small work in a country which has, mostly, finished with great work. They have been given the world back as a quiet place. I cannot tell you in advance whether you will be one of them. I do not know who you will be when you read this. I know who you are now, at twenty-three. I know that the country you have entered is a country that is going to ask a great deal of you and give you very little in return. I hope, in the years between now and when you read this, that you will have been kept by something. A book, a person, a small habit. Anything. The kept can become keepers. They cannot do it from nothing. They have to have been kept first.

If you have come to the cottage, then something kept you. I do not need to know what. The walking that brought you here was the kept-ness becoming visible to you. The walking was the laying down. You have been laying it down for a hundred miles or two hundred. The cottage is the place where, if you wish, you can begin to learn to be a keeper.

It is a small life. There is bread to bake. There is peat to cut. There is a roof to keep. There are deer at dusk on certain mornings. There is a window upstairs. There are books in the second drawer of this chest, although you have not yet opened that drawer, and I will leave them for you to find on your own. There is no great work. There is only the small work. It is enough. It has always been enough. It will be enough for you.

If you do not stay, my boy, that is also well. The walking has done its work either way. You may go on. You may go down to the boat with Donal, who will still be alive when you read this, I am almost sure, and you may cross back, and you may take the small things you have learned and carry them somewhere else. Wherever you carry them they will be the same small things. The cottage is not the only place to be a keeper. The keeping is the keeping. The cottage is only one of its rooms.

I am tired now. I have not written so much in many years. I will not write much more. I send you my love, my boy. I send you also the deer, who do come down. They came down this morning. They came down and one of them looked up at my window for a moment and saw me. There were three of them. They will come down whether you stay or go. They will come down whether the world keeps speaking or stops. They are why we kept the cottage. The cottage was always for them. We kept it so that there would always be one window from which a creature like you, my dear boy, could see them at dusk.

Be small. Be slow. Listen. Eat your bread.

Your grandmother.

· · ·

I sat on the boards for a long time after I had read it the second time. The dog had come closer and was lying with his chin on my thigh and his eyes on my face. The rain ran down the small window in the gable. The fire below in the kitchen had burned down. I did not go down to put more on it.

I read it a third time, with my hand resting on the dog's head. I read it more slowly. I did not weep. I had wept in the glen and on the road up Loch Lomond, and the weeping was, by then, a thing I had largely concluded. I read it the third time the way one reads a thing one has been given that is enough.

I folded the letter at last. I put it back into the bottom drawer, on top of the loose papers, where I had found it. I would read it again, perhaps, on other days. There would be days. There would be many days. I closed the drawer.

I went down to the kitchen. I built up the fire. I put the kettle on the range. The rain ran down the window. The dog came down the stair after me and lay in his place on the warm flags. I cut a slice of bread and ate it slowly, standing at the window, looking out at the grey rain and the grey bay below.

I had not made up my mind. I would not make it up that day. I would not make it up that week. The cottage did not require me to. The cottage went on with what it was doing. I went on with what I was doing, which was eating bread at a window in the rain. The two of us, the cottage and I, were doing the small work, together, for now. It was enough. It was, as she had said, enough. It had always been enough. I had simply been a long time in the noise, and the noise had been telling me, for nineteen years, that it was not enough, and the noise had been wrong about a great many things, and this was perhaps the one it had been most wrong about of all.

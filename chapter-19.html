Northbound

Chapter Nineteen

Glencoe

I went up into Glencoe on a morning of low cloud and a fine cold rain that did not quite fall but hung in the air. The track climbed slowly through low country at first, between bog and birch, and then more steeply as the mountains came down on both sides and shut out the sky. By midday I was in the glen proper, with the great walls of the Three Sisters above me on the south side and the long ridge of the Aonach Eagach on the north, and the rain easing, and the cloud beginning to lift off the tops in slow torn pieces.

I had not seen mountains of this scale before. I had thought I had. I had been wrong. There is a difference between a hill that one can imagine walking up and a mountain that does not let one imagine such a thing. The Three Sisters did not let one imagine such a thing. They were not the kind of mountains a man walks up. They were the kind a man stands at the foot of, and looks up at, and is given a small accurate sense of his own size, and walks on.

The dog had grown very quiet in the glen. He stayed close. He did not run ahead. He looked up, often, at the mountains, with his head tipped slightly. He was not afraid. He was attending. The country was a country that asked attention of any creature in it, and he was giving it. I did the same.

· · ·

I walked through the glen all afternoon. The wind came down the corries in slow strong pulses and the cloud lifted off the mountains in slow strong pulses and the light came and went in slow strong pulses, and the country worked itself in front of me as though I were watching a very great machine that had nothing to do with men, that had been doing its work since before there had been men in the country, and would go on doing its work after the men, and was perhaps a little more itself now that the men were gone, and was a little more visible to me because I had perhaps become a little more myself in the walking.

I had stopped, since the loch, weeping at all. The weeping had been done in its own time, on the road up the eastern shore, and had concluded itself. I had thought I was done with it. I was not. In the glen, in the early afternoon, I came round a bend in the track and the upper part of the south wall of the glen opened to me, the great corrie, the long buttress, the stream falling from a hanging valley high up in white threads against the dark rock, and I stopped, and I sat down on a stone, and I wept again, although I could not have told anyone what I was weeping for.

I think I was weeping for the country. I think I had been carrying, since the building of glass, a small dim shame for the country I had been part of, the country that had built buildings of glass, the country that had bought coffee in paper cups and thrown the cups away, the country that had ridden in trains without looking out of the windows. I had been carrying that shame in some small place in my chest and I had not known I had been carrying it. The country itself, the country of mountains and rivers and forests, had not asked me to carry it. The country had been here all along, doing its work, regardless. The shame had been mine for myself.

It came out of me on the stone in the glen. It came out as a kind of weeping that did not have words for itself. It went on for some long while. The dog sat in front of me and watched. He did not press himself against me. He did not try to comfort. He sat in front of me and he watched me with his slow attentive face, and I understood that he was doing what Iona had taught me on the bench, which is the thing you do when another creature is having a difficult hour. You do not interrupt. You do not ease. You sit with them, in their hour, and you let the hour be theirs, and you wait.

The hour was mine. It ended in its own time. I wiped my face with the back of my hand. I drank from my flask. I rose. The dog rose. I looked up at the corrie. The cloud had gone off it. The light was on the wet rock and the rock was almost black, with white threads of water still falling from the hanging valley.

"Thank you," I said to it. I said it quietly. The mountain did not answer, of course. But I was not really speaking to the mountain. I was speaking to the part of myself that had let me sit on the stone and weep without asking why. That part had not been there before. That part was new. I thanked it as one thanks the appearance of a friend one had not expected.

We walked on.

· · ·

I camped that night near the head of the glen, in a flat place by a burn, in the lee of a stand of birch that had somehow seeded itself in the great rock country and held on. I made a small fire. I cooked the last of Iona's porridge. The dog ate from my hand. The cloud came down again as evening came on, and the tops of the mountains went into it, and the world narrowed to the small bright place around the fire and the dim circle of the birches.

I had been walking for what I now thought must be the better part of five months. The face I would have seen in a mirror, had I had one, would not have been the face that had left the building of glass. I had noticed this the few times I had passed a window with any reflective glass in it and had glanced up. The face was thinner. The hair was longer and was now mostly grey, although it had been grey before, only carefully kept dark. The eyes were not the eyes of the man in the building of glass. I could not have said what was different about them. They were perhaps quieter. They were perhaps less in a hurry. They had perhaps been finding things I had not been letting them find for a long time.

The dog laid his head on my thigh by the fire. I put my hand on his head.

"Tomorrow we go down," I said. "Tomorrow we are at the sea-loch."

He looked up at me. He looked into the fire. He sighed in his old long way.

I thought about my grandmother's house. I thought about the kitchen window that gave out onto water. I thought about the deer that came down to drink. I thought about the small white cottage on the island. I had been thinking of these things for a hundred and fifty days. They had grown in me into a place that was perhaps not very like the place I would find. The place I would find would be the place. The place in me would be the place I had walked toward. The two places had perhaps very little to do with each other and would have very little to do with each other when I came to the shore. I was prepared for this, or had told myself I was. Edmund had been right. The shape the walking had made of me was the answer the walking had given. The shape of the shore would be the world's answer, and the two answers were not the same kind of thing, and one ought not to ask one to do the work of the other.

I lay back. The cloud was so low that I could not see the stars. The fire kept us warm enough. The dog moved closer. The burn ran by us in its bed of stones. I slept.

I dreamed that night, for the first time in many weeks, of the building of glass. I was in the lift. The lift was going up. There was a small polite chime as we passed each floor. There were other people in the lift, although I could not see their faces. The lift was going to a floor I had been going to for nineteen years. I knew, in the dream, that when the doors opened I would step out, and I would walk down the long corridor with the carpet on it that had a particular pattern of grey diamonds, and I would sit at my desk, and I would look at a screen that had a great many small numbers on it. I knew this in the way one knows things in a dream, with a small heavy weight in the middle of the chest.

The lift came to my floor. The chime sounded. The doors slid open.

Outside the doors there was a hill of bracken running up to a sky of low cloud. The corridor was not there. There was no carpet. There was a path winding up through the bracken. The dog was at the top of the hill, looking back at me.

I stepped out.

I woke, in the glen, in the dim grey first light of a morning in which the cloud had still not lifted. I lay for a while looking up at it. I thought: the lift had taken me to the right floor at last. I thought: I had been getting off at the wrong floor for nineteen years. The dog stirred against my hip. The burn ran on. The day was beginning. I rose, and I rolled my groundsheet, and I shouldered the pack, and we walked on down out of Glencoe toward the sea.

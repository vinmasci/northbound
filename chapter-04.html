Northbound

Chapter Four

A Distant Figure

We came down off the moor and into the long pale country of the Dales. The fields rose and fell in green and yellow squares cut by the low grey lines of the dry-stone walls, and the walls climbed up over the shoulders of the hills and stood there against the sky as they had stood for some hundreds of years. There were sheep in the fields. There were a great many sheep. They had escaped through gaps in the walls in places and gone wandering, and lambs that had been born in the spring were no longer lambs but were not yet sheep, and they cropped the long grass and looked at us with their slot-pupilled eyes and were not very afraid.

The dog, who had a herding line in him somewhere, found this difficult. He would crouch when he saw a knot of them and his eyes would go bright and his shoulders would set, and I would have to call him with the small click of my tongue and he would come, but reluctantly. The work was in him. He did not know what to do with it now that there was no one to ask it of him.

I knew what that was like. We walked on together, the two of us with our useless old skills, down the white road between the green fields.

· · ·

I had been a man with a job. I had done it for a long time. I had been good at it, in the way one is good at a thing when one has not asked oneself in some years whether it ought to be done. I had sat in a building of glass and had moved numbers from one column to another, and other people had moved them back, and at the end of each year there were more of them, and we had been pleased. I had risen each morning at six and had stood under hot water and had put on a shirt of a particular weave, and had eaten a piece of toast standing at a counter, and had ridden a train. I had done this for nineteen years.

I do not know now what I would say if you asked me what the work had been for. I had said many things at the time. I had given good answers. I had believed some of them. The answers had gone, and the work had gone, and the building of glass had gone, and the train had gone, and I was walking up a lane in the Yorkshire Dales between two stone walls, and a dog who had no name was at my heel, and the answers had not come back to me, and I had stopped expecting them to.

The thing I noticed, as I walked, was that I was not unhappy. This was, in its way, the strangest of all the things I had noticed since I had begun walking. I had been unhappy in the building of glass for nineteen years, although I had not used the word. I had used other words. I had used tired, and busy, and fine. I had used these words to other people and to myself. Now I had stopped using them. I had not replaced them with anything. I had only stopped, and the stopping had let in a quietness that was not happiness exactly but was the ground in which happiness, perhaps, might one day grow.

· · ·

It was in the middle of that afternoon that I saw the figure on the ridge.

The road ran along the side of a long hill, and across the dale on the opposite ridge, perhaps two miles distant, a single shape stood against the sky. I stopped. The dog stopped. I shaded my eyes with my hand.

It was a person. I was sure of this immediately, although the figure was small and dark against the bright sky. I could not tell anything else about them. I could not tell whether they had seen me. I could not tell whether they were facing toward me or away. They stood very still on the ridge for what felt like a long time and was perhaps in fact only a minute, and then they walked on along the line of the ridge and went down on the far side and were gone.

I stood in the road and looked at the place on the ridge where the figure had been. The dog leaned against my leg. My heart was doing a thing in my chest I had not expected. I had thought that I had grown accustomed to the absence of others. I had thought that I was, by this point in my walking, a person who had taken his own company as sufficient. The pulse in my neck was telling me something different. The pulse in my neck was telling me that I had been waiting all along.

I did not call out. The figure was too far. The wind was the wrong way. And, too, I had a feeling that to call out would be to ask a question that was not yet mine to ask. I stood and watched the empty ridge for a while longer, and then I started down the lane toward the dale bottom, where there was a beck and a bridge and the road that crossed it, and I thought that if the figure had also come down from the ridge we might meet somewhere along the way.

We did not meet.

I crossed the beck. I climbed the far slope. I went up onto the ridge where the figure had been and I stood in the place I judged they had stood, and there was nothing to mark it. The grass was bent in no particular way. There were no footprints in the soft places. There was a small pile of stones that might have been a cairn or might have been a thing put there by a child a hundred years ago. I sat by the cairn with my back against it and I looked at the country I had come up through, the long pale road, the sheep in their squares, the white dust of the dry-stone walls, and below me on the road I had walked I saw nothing moving at all, not even a bird.

I thought: they did not want to be met. I thought: I am being given to know that someone is here, and I am being given to know that they have chosen, today, not to come to me. The respect of it touched me. It was the same respect, I thought, that had been in the patched window of the petrol station, and the locked door of the church, and the kettle put back on the hob in the kitchen with the apple and the spoon. A people who left things as they found them, including each other.

The dog had come up behind me and was standing beside the cairn looking out over the dale. I put my hand on his head. He looked at me sideways without moving his head, the way he did when he was content.

"Well," I said. It was the first word I had spoken aloud in a great many days, and it came out cracked and small. The dog turned his head fully and looked at me. I did not know what I had meant by it. I had only meant, perhaps, to put a single small noise into the wide silent country and see how it sat there. It sat there well enough. It sat there as though it had always belonged.

· · ·

That night we camped in a fold of the ridge a little way down from the cairn, where there was a spring and a flat place. I made a small fire of the dead heather. I cooked porridge with the last of the oats from Tebay and ate it without sweetness. The dog had eaten earlier, having taken a rabbit at dusk in his quiet professional way.

I sat by the fire and looked across the dark dale toward the place where the road I had walked must lie. There were no lights anywhere. There had not been lights for a long time. The stars came out and came out and came out until the sky was crowded with them, and I lay back in the heather and looked at them. I had not, in nineteen years in the building of glass, looked at them more than perhaps three or four times. They had been there the whole while. The thought made me laugh once, a short bark of a laugh, and the dog turned his head.

I thought again of the figure on the ridge. I thought: there are others. Perhaps not many. Perhaps very few. But there are others. They are walking too, or staying, or doing whatever it is they have decided to do. Some of them will come to me in time. Some of them will not. Some of them I will go to. The world is not empty. It is only quiet.

And then I thought, half on the edge of sleep: they were given the chance and most of them took it. And the figure on the ridge had not. And nor had I. And nor, I supposed, had the writer of the notebook, although she had perhaps been writing about something else, or to someone else, and I had read her wrongly. There was no way to know. There would be more reading to do. There would be more figures on more ridges. There would be more locked doors and more open ones, and I would walk on.

I slept with the dog warm against my back, and the fire going down to embers, and the great stars overhead in their patient houses, and I dreamed of nothing that I remembered.

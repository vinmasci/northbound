Northbound

Chapter Twelve

Walenty

The farm was called Cairnfold, although I did not know this for some hours after I arrived. There was no sign at the gate. The gate stood open. There was a yard of cobbles worn smooth by long use, with a stone trough on one side fed by a pipe coming through the wall of a byre, and the trough was full of clear water and there were two collies drinking at it as I came up. They lifted their heads when they saw me. They stood very still. They were proper working collies, lean and watchful, with the eye that such dogs have. They did not bark.

My dog stopped in the gateway and sat down. He looked at the two collies and the two collies looked at him, and the three of them held an inquiry of their own among themselves that I did not interrupt. After a moment one of the collies came forward a few steps and sniffed at the air toward my dog, and my dog rose and walked into the yard at my side, and the inquiry was finished. The collies returned to their drinking.

A man came round the corner of the byre with a bucket in each hand. He was perhaps forty-five, very lean, with a long face weathered to the colour of brown earth, and pale grey eyes, and he wore a wax jacket much patched and a flat cap that had been a flat cap for a long time. He set the buckets down in the yard when he saw me. He did not look surprised. The collies had not barked. He had perhaps known I was coming for some time, by their not barking.

"Good day," he said. His voice had a slight lift in it that was not Scottish. He said the word day with a small j in it.

"Good day," I said.

"You are walking."

"Yes."

"From south."

"Yes."

"Far?"

"A long way."

He nodded. He looked at the dog. The dog sat down beside my left boot and looked back at him politely. He nodded again, as though he had asked the dog the same questions and had received the same answers.

"Come in," he said. "I am Walenty. Have you eaten?"

"Not since this morning."

"Then you will eat now," he said. He picked up one of his buckets. "Bring this," he said, gesturing at the other.

I picked it up. It was milk. It was warm. I followed him across the yard with the bucket of warm milk in my hand and the dog at my heel and the two collies coming behind us at a respectful distance, and we went in by a low door at the back of the farmhouse into a kitchen with a stone-flagged floor and a black range and the smell, after the long road, of bread and butter and something that had been simmering in a pot for most of the day.

He set me at a table and gave me a bowl of barley stew with mutton in it, and a heel of bread, and a glass of the warm milk, and he sat opposite me and watched me eat without speaking. He did not eat himself. I had the impression he had eaten earlier and had only sat to keep me company. The dog received a bowl on the floor by the range. The two collies lay down at the door of the kitchen and watched the dog eat with the impassivity of professionals observing the work of another.

When I had finished he took the bowl away and brought me a slice of pie that had apples in it and a great deal of butter in the pastry, and a second glass of milk, and I ate the pie and drank the milk, and at last I sat back and looked at him and said, "Thank you."

He nodded. He poured himself a glass of the milk and drank half of it slowly.

"Walenty," I said.

"Walenty Sobczak."

"You have been here a long time."

"Twenty-three years," he said. "I came as a young man. I came to the farm of the McRae family. I worked for them. They were good people. The father died, then the mother died, and there was a son who did not want a farm, he was an architect in Edinburgh, but he kept the place because he could not bear to sell it, and he said to me, Walenty you can stay here as long as you like, you can do as you like. I stayed. He went to Edinburgh. He came at Christmas and at Easter and in the summer for a week. He was a good man. I farmed for him. He paid me only what he could. It did not matter. I had nowhere I needed to go and money is for going. I stayed."

"And after."

"After," he said. He looked into his milk. He set the glass down. "After, I am still here. He is not. The summer when it came, he was here, he was here for the summer week. He was a good man. He was kind to his mother. He did not deserve more than another man, but he did not deserve less. He had been good to me. He went out one morning and walked up the hill. I knew him. I knew his walking. He walked up the hill the way a man walks who is going up the hill for the last time. I let him go. I did not call after him. He had not called after his father when his father went. He had not called after his mother. It is not a thing one calls after."

"He heard it," I said.

"He heard it," Walenty said. "Yes. They all heard it. Almost all. Everyone in the valley. The McGintys at the next farm down. The Camerons. Even the children. They went one by one. Some of them in pairs. The Camerons all went together, the whole family, on a Tuesday I think. They walked up the hill where the McRae son had walked. They did not come back."

"You did not."

"No."

"Why."

He looked at me for a moment with his pale grey eyes. He looked at me steadily, without urgency, in the way of a man who has been asked the question many times and has each time answered it, and has perhaps each time given a slightly different answer, although the truth was the same in each.

"I heard it too," he said. "Of course I heard it. Everyone heard it. I am not a special man, my ears are not special. But I will tell you what I heard, which is perhaps not what others heard. I heard a long note coming down. I heard it coming down a long curve of itself, like falling. A long quiet falling. It went into me. It said something to me without words and what it said was: you are tired, you have been tired for a long time, you can lay it down now, you can come." He paused. "Do you understand?"

"I think so."

"Good. It said this. To everyone perhaps it said this. I do not know what it said to others. I know what it said to me. And I sat in this kitchen and I listened to it, and I thought: I am tired. It is true. I am tired. I have been tired for a long time. It was a great relief that something at last had said this to me. The voice of the thing was a kind voice. It was not a cruel voice. It was a voice that loved the world, I think, and was tired for the world, and was perhaps offering to take the world's tiredness up. I sat in this kitchen for a long time with the voice."

"And then?"

"And then," he said, "I thought of the cows."

I did not laugh. I had felt my mouth begin to make a smile because of how he had said it, the small dry lift in his voice, but I did not laugh. He had not meant it as a joke. He looked at me steadily.

"The cows were in the lower field," he said. "There were eleven of them. They had been milked that morning. They would need milking again that evening. They could not milk themselves. Two of them were old, one was lame, one had a calf that was three weeks old. The dogs were in the yard. The dogs needed feeding. The chickens needed shutting in at dusk because of the foxes. There was a great deal of work that needed doing in the next twelve hours, and there was no one to do it but me. The McRae son had gone up the hill. The McGintys had gone. The Camerons had gone. The voice was telling me I could lay it down. But I could not lay it down. The cows were there. The cows had not heard the voice. The cows did not know."

"So you stayed."

"I stayed for the cows," he said. "For that day. I told myself, only for that day. I will milk them this evening, and shut the chickens, and then I will sit, and I will see if the voice is still there. I milked the cows. I shut the chickens. I came in. I made a small supper. I sat in this chair, in this kitchen. The voice was thinner, but it was there. I sat with it. I thought: tomorrow there will again be cows, and chickens, and dogs. I thought: also, there will be the rain, and the grass, and the wind on the hill. There will be the bread that I will need to make, because I had used the last of the loaf at supper. There will be the small things. The voice will be there or not. The small things will be there. I will choose the small things, because they are choosing me, and the voice is only asking."

"And the voice."

"It went," he said. "Slowly. It is gone now. I do not hear it. Sometimes there is a thinness in the air that I think is the place it used to be. But the voice itself, no. It went. Perhaps it gave up on me. Perhaps it was always going to go from those who did not answer. I do not know. I am not a clever man. I work the farm. I tend the cows. I make the bread. I pass."

He took a fiddle from a hook by the door after we had cleared the table together. He sat by the range and he played for perhaps an hour. He played slow tunes I did not know, and one I did, an old air called Mist Covered Mountains that my grandmother had used to hum at her stove. He played without ornament. He played as a man saws wood. He played the tunes through and let them end and went on to the next without speaking. The dog and the two collies lay together on the flagstones in front of the range, all three of them in a single warm heap, the dog's head on one collie's flank, the other collie with its chin on the dog's foreleg. I had not known he could lie like that with strange dogs. I had not known a great many things about him. I sat at the table and listened to Walenty play.

I thought, while he played: there is a noise the voice cannot pass through. The cataloguer's pencil. The priest's bread. The bee woman's lavender. The fiddle in the kitchen of a Polish farmhand on a hill in Galloway. The cows in the lower field who do not know. They are all of them small noises, very small, and the voice is a great voice, but the small noises are made with care, and the great voice is not. The small noises are made by hands that know what they love. That is the difference, perhaps. That is all the difference there is.

He stopped playing at length. He laid the fiddle on his knee and looked at the fire.

"Stay tonight," he said. "Stay tomorrow if you like. I will not say longer. I think you are a man who will go on. But stay tonight."

"Thank you," I said.

"There is a bed in the back room," he said. "There are sheets. I will give you bread for the road. I will give you cheese. I will not give you milk. Milk does not travel."

"Walenty," I said.

"Yes."

"Thank you."

He looked at me. He nodded once, slowly, the way a man nods who is accepting a thing on behalf of all the cows and the chickens and the dogs, and the long evenings in the kitchen, and the small noise he has made against the great voice for some long time now.

"Good," he said. "Sleep, then. The morning will come early. They always come early here."

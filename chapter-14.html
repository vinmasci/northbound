Northbound

Chapter Fourteen

Stillness

I stayed three days at the cottage on the cliff, although she had said one or two. Neither of us mentioned the third. She did not ask me to leave on the second morning and I did not offer. The day went on as the day before had gone on. There was the pattern of the house and we both moved within it without negotiation.

The pattern was this. She rose before light. I would wake to the small sounds of her moving in the kitchen below, the tap, the kettle, the iron lifter on the stove top, the soft padding of her bare feet on the flags. By the time I came down she would have gone out, and there would be a pot of tea on the table under a knitted cosy, and a loaf and butter and three eggs in a small blue bowl, and she would have left a single fresh egg on top of the pile. I would eat. I would look out of the kitchen window at the long firth, which at dawn was a different colour each morning and which I had begun to wait for. After I had eaten I would go out into the yard with the dog, and split wood for an hour, or carry water, or whatever there was to do. There was always something. She did not give me jobs. I found them. The house had been a long time without a man's strength in it and there were small repairs everywhere that wanted doing, and I did them, slowly, in order, beginning with what was nearest the door and working my way out.

She would come back in the late morning from wherever she had gone, with a basket, perhaps, or a coil of rope, or once a small fish. We would eat at noon. We did not always speak at the meal. Sometimes one of us said a thing. Sometimes neither of us said anything for the full half-hour we were at the table. She would clear the plates and I would help her wash them, although she did not ask, and we would dry them together with a single tea-towel passed between us, and put them on the dresser, and the day's hinge would turn there and the afternoon would begin.

In the afternoons I read or I walked. I had picked up the small green book that the cataloguer had given me again, in that house, and I read it more slowly than I had read anything for many years. There was a chair under a window in the room where I slept, and I would sit in it with the book on my knee and the dog at my feet, and I would read a page, and stop, and look out at the firth, and read another page, and stop. I had read perhaps six pages on the first afternoon. Five on the second. By the third afternoon I had begun to see that the book was not a thing one read in the way I had been reading things, the way I had read in the building of glass on the train going to the building of glass and on the train coming back. The book was a thing one read at the speed of the writing, which was the speed of a particular woman's particular walking on a particular mountain in a particular weather. To read faster than that speed was to take less than half of what was offered. To read at that speed was to walk the mountain with her for a few minutes at a time, and to come away each evening with a little more of the mountain in oneself.

I had not known, until those afternoons in the chair by the window, that books had been giving me half of what they offered for nineteen years and that I had not noticed.

· · ·

On the second evening I asked Iona where she went in the mornings.

"To the rocks," she said. "There is a place a little way along under the cliff. I sit there. I look at the water. I do not do anything. I sit and look at the water for as long as I am needed there."

"Needed by what."

She thought about this. She held a piece of bread in her hand and turned it over slowly and looked at it without seeing it. Her face had the still quality in the lamplight that it had had in the afternoon at the gate.

"I do not know," she said. "I have stopped trying to know. I had thought, when I first started doing it, that I was doing it for myself. I was sad. I had lost people. I sat and looked at the water and I thought it was a private kind of medicine. After a while I understood it was not for me. It is for the place. The place needs to be looked at by someone, every morning. I do not know why. The place did not say. But it needs it, and I am here, and I do it. It is my work, perhaps. It is what I have instead of bees."

I looked at her.

"Have you tried not going."

"Once," she said. "On a morning when I was unwell. The day was wrong. The whole day was wrong. The sea looked wrong. The air felt wrong. I went out in the afternoon for an hour and sat there to make up for it and the place forgave me. I have not tried again. It does not like missing. I do not know if that is true. It is what it feels like to me. I am not a clever woman. I do not know how to say it better."

"You are very clear," I said.

"That is not the same as clever," she said. "It is sometimes the opposite."

The third morning I went with her to the rocks. She did not invite me. I had risen earlier than my usual and had been sitting in the kitchen drinking the tea she had left, and when she came in from the yard with her basket she looked at me and said, "If you like." I had said yes before I had thought about it.

The path went down off the cliff a quarter of a mile to the west, by a steep cut in the rock that had perhaps been a smugglers' way once, and it brought us out onto a small shelf of stone above the high tide line. There was a flat slab there in the angle of two larger rocks, with a stone behind it that fitted the back, and Iona sat in this place as a person sits in a chair that has been made for them. She did not invite me to sit beside her. I understood that there was only the one place. I sat on a smaller boulder a few yards off, with the dog at my feet, and I looked out at the firth, which was the first colour of the dawn.

The sun came up behind us out of the land. The water went from grey to gold and back to grey. A heron stood on a rock far out and did not move for the whole of the time we were there. A gull came and went. The tide turned somewhere I could not see and began its long return. I did not look at Iona. She did not look at me. We sat on the rocks for what I think was an hour, although I could not have said. When she rose I rose. We went back up the cut without speaking, and the dog followed.

At the top of the cliff she stopped and turned to me.

"There," she said. "That is what I do. That is what is needed. You may understand it now or you may understand it later. It does not matter. I wanted you to see it. I do not show it to many."

"Thank you," I said.

"It will help you with the walking," she said. "When you are tired. When you are not sure why you are walking. Remember a person sat on a rock for an hour and looked at a firth and did not do anything. The world is held up by such hours. The world has always been held up by such hours. We had forgotten. Some of us are remembering."

· · ·

That night, after supper, she came and sat beside me on the long bench under the kitchen window and she put her hand on the back of my hand where it lay on my knee, and she did not say anything. We sat like that for a long time. The dog was asleep on the mat by the door. The lamp was low. The fire in the range had gone down to a red core. There was no question and there was no answer between us. Her hand was warm and dry. The bones in it were small. After some time she lifted her hand from mine and stood up and went to the dresser and took down a candle in a holder and lit it from the lamp and went out of the kitchen and up the stair to her own room. She did not look back. I sat for a while longer. Then I went into the small room at the end of the corridor where my pack was, and I lay down in the bed.

I lay awake for some long time. I did not know what had happened. I thought perhaps nothing had happened. Then I thought perhaps something had happened that I had no language for, and that the lack of language did not lessen the thing. I thought of her sitting beside me, and her hand on the back of my hand, and the absence of any statement. I thought of how, in the building of glass, I would have made a statement. I would have said the word love. I would have asked the question are we. I would have proposed a meal in a restaurant. I would have, in the end and after some weeks, broken whatever the small thing had been that was forming, by speaking it before it had a shape. The small thing on the bench had had no language. It had been allowed not to have one. It was perhaps the kindest thing anyone had ever done for me, although I would not have been able to say what it was if pressed.

I slept, in the end, and dreamed of the heron on the rock far out in the firth, which had not moved.

· · ·

On the morning of the fourth day I came down with my pack. She was at the kitchen table with her tea. The loaf was on the board. Three eggs in the blue bowl. She did not look surprised. She nodded at the chair across from her. I sat. She poured my tea.

"You have decided," she said.

"Yes."

"Good," she said. "I would have asked you today, if you had not. I would have done it gently."

"I know."

"Eat," she said.

I ate.

"I will give you a thing or two for the road," she said. "Bread, of course. Cheese. Apples from the tree at the back. There is a knife I want you to have. It was my grandmother's. She brought it from the islands. It is a good knife. It is sharper than it looks. I will wrap it for you so you do not cut your pack."

"I do not need a knife," I said.

"You will," she said. "Where you are going."

I looked up at her. She was buttering a piece of bread.

"You know where I am going."

"I know the country," she said. "I know the loch. I know the island. I will write you down a way to it. The way I would go. There are perhaps better ways. There are not many people now who would tell you. I will tell you the one I know."

"Iona."

"Yes."

"How do you know."

She did not answer at once. She set the buttered bread on a plate and pushed it across the table to me. She drank from her cup. She looked at me over the rim of it.

"The cottage on the island," she said. "The one with the deer that come down. I know of it. People know of it, the ones who refused. It is on a list, of a kind. It is not written down. We hold the list among us. There are perhaps eight or nine such places. Yours is one. You are walking to one of them. I think you do not know that you are. You think you are walking to a place you remember from your childhood. The two are perhaps the same place. I do not know. I think they perhaps are."

I set down my cup. My hand was not quite steady.

"Eat," she said. "You will need it for the road. There are six more days of walking, by my way. I will tell you all of them. You will go up the coast first. You will turn inland at Stranraer. Eat your bread. We have time."

I ate my bread. The dog lay at my feet. The firth outside the window was the first colour of the dawn.

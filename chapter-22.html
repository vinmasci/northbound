Northbound

Chapter Twenty-Two

The Crossing

The crossing took us most of the morning. Donal did not put the small sail up. There was no wind to take it. He rowed steadily with his long pulls and now and again he would rest the oars across the thwart and take a drink of water from a stoneware bottle and look at the water around us, and look at the sun, and look at the small green shape on the horizon, and pick up the oars again. I had offered to row. He had said, perhaps later, perhaps not. He did not give it up.

The water beyond the mouth of the loch was a different water from the water inside it. It was a darker blue. It moved differently. It had a long slow swell that lifted the boat and let it down again in a great patient rhythm, like the breathing of a creature one was riding on. The small wooden hull creaked at each lift. The dog stood in the bow with his front paws against the gunwale and his nose into the wind that we made by our own moving, and he was happier in this hour than I had seen him in any hour of our walking. He had become, in some way I had not noticed, a sea dog. Perhaps he had always been one.

We went west. The mainland fell away behind us. The mountains of the mainland stood blue and grey over the water for a long time and then became thinner and bluer and more like the idea of mountains than mountains themselves. Ahead, the small green shape grew. It became a hill in the middle of a long flat coastal land. It became, after another half-hour, two hills, with a low saddle between. It became, after another half-hour again, a coast, a shore, a fringe of pale rock at the edge of the water, and a green country rising behind, and somewhere in that green country, although I could not yet see them, two small white cottages set far apart at opposite ends.

"Are there other people," I said.

"On the island."

"Yes."

"My wife. The deer. The seals. There is a colt of red deer on the hill that I have been watching grow up for three years. He is a fine creature. He will be a great stag in a few more years if he keeps his head down."

"And the cottages."

"Two cottages," he said. "My wife's, at the south end. Your grandmother's, at the north. There were others, very long ago. Three or four. They are gone. The stones of them are still on the ground. The grass has come over them. The walls are low. You will see when you walk."

I did not ask whether my grandmother had been on the island in any of his crossings. I had a small dread of the answer to either form of the question, the yes or the no. I let it sit. He would tell me what he wanted to tell me. He had been clear. The walking had earned me the knowing.

· · ·

It was perhaps an hour off the island that we saw the dolphins.

I had heard they came in along these coasts and I had not believed them. I had thought they were the imaginings of postcards. I had been wrong about so many things in this country that I should not have been surprised to be wrong about this. They came up alongside the boat in a small group of four, perhaps five, moving in their easy long arcs out of the water and back into it, the dorsal fins black against the blue water, and they did not seem in a hurry. They were not racing the boat. They were not playing with us, perhaps, or playing with us only as a small side-project. They came up alongside and went under and came up on the other side and looped, and went under and came up ahead, and one of them, the largest, lifted its eye out of the water for a moment and looked at me as it passed.

I have thought about that look. I cannot describe it. The eye was a black eye in a grey face, and the eye looked at me as the stag had looked at me in the Trossachs, with the same long calm consideration, the same unsurprised acknowledgement, the same regard. I had been looked at, in nineteen years in the building of glass, by many human eyes, and not one of them had looked at me as the stag had looked at me, or as the dolphin did now in the cold water beside the boat. The dolphins of the building of glass had been the wrong creatures.

The dolphin slipped under the surface. The group went on west, perhaps a hundred yards, then turned south, and was gone.

I sat on the thwart and did not speak. Donal rowed. The dog had watched the dolphins with a great attentive stillness. He had not barked. He had not tried to follow. He had stood in the bow with his ears forward and watched them, and when they had gone he had turned his head and looked at me, briefly, and then turned forward again. He had agreed with me about them. We had been in agreement about a great many things on this journey. The dog was the best companion I had ever had, and I include in that the people I had loved.

· · ·

The island when we came up to it had a small inlet of pale sand at the north end, with a low rock breakwater built across it, and a small wooden jetty that someone had been keeping in repair. Donal rowed us up to it. The dog jumped off onto the jetty before the boat had touched. He went up to the head of the jetty and stood, and looked back at me, and waited.

I climbed out. Donal stayed in the boat. He took up the oars and pulled her out a little way from the jetty and set the small anchor and let her swing. He looked at me across the few yards of water.

"I will be here," he said. "Take what time you need."

"Donal."

"Yes."

"Thank you."

"You will thank me later," he said. "If you want to. I do not require it. Go on."

I shouldered the small pack. The dog and I walked to the head of the jetty.

And then I saw them.

They had come down to the shore on the other side of the inlet, perhaps three hundred yards away across the pale sand. There were six of them. They were red deer. There were three hinds and two yearlings and one stag with a heavy clean rack of horns, and they were standing in a small loose group at the edge of the water, and the largest hind had her head lowered to drink. They had not seen us. The wind, what little there was, was off them and toward us. They were the small thing in the morning my grandmother had told me about, in the kitchen long ago, with the smell of peat and wet wool around her. On certain mornings, she had said, and her hand had been on my head, and I had been six years old, on certain mornings the deer come down to drink.

I stood at the head of the jetty and I watched them.

The dog stood beside me. He did not move. He had become, in the last few hours, a creature who would not move when something needed not to be moved at. He sat down at last, very slowly, beside my left foot, and watched the deer drink.

The hind drank for perhaps a minute. She lifted her head. Water ran down her chin. She turned her head and looked along the shore. The stag lifted his head. The yearlings did not lift theirs. The other hinds drank. After a moment the largest hind walked a few paces along the sand, looking, and then walked back, and lowered her head to drink again. None of them had seen us. Or none of them had decided to see us. Perhaps they had seen us and had decided we were not worth seeing. I could not tell.

I did not move. I did not breathe much. The dog did not move either. We stood at the head of the jetty for what felt like a long time. The deer drank. The deer, having drunk, turned, and walked together up the shore toward the green country behind, and went between two low rocks at the top of the beach, and were gone.

I let out a long breath. The dog looked up at me. I looked back at the boat in the inlet. Donal was sitting in the bow with his pipe, looking out at the water, although whether he had seen the deer or not I could not say. I think he had. I think he had been looking at them as I had been looking at them, although in his own private way, and he had perhaps seen them many times before, and he had seen them this time as well, because some things do not get less themselves for being seen many times. I think this is true. I have come to believe it.

· · ·

The path from the jetty went up between the low rocks at the top of the beach and into the green country. It was a faint path, perhaps an old animal-track that someone had once walked enough to make a footing of it, although not for some long time. The grass had grown over it in places. I went up the path. The dog walked at my heel.

The path took me through a low stand of windbent thorn and then up onto open ground that rose gently for half a mile to a small rise. From the top of the rise the path went down again into a small green hollow that had the sea on one side and the sheltering hill on the other, and there, in the hollow, set into the grass with its back against the hill and its front looking out west across a small bay to the open sea, was the cottage.

It was very small. It was whitewashed, with a slate roof, and a single chimney at one end, and a door painted black, and two small windows on either side of the door. There was a low wall of stones around it that had been a garden wall once and was now mostly overgrown with thrift and sea-pinks. There was a small stone shed at the side. There was no garden, properly. There was only the grass and the thrift and the wall.

The chimney had no smoke coming out of it. The door was closed. The windows were dim, with no lamp visible inside.

I stood at the top of the rise and looked down at the cottage for a long time. The dog sat beside me. He had become very still. The wind off the sea moved his coat. There was no sound except the wind and the soft far sound of the sea on the rocks. There were no birds at this hour. There were no deer.

"All right," I said.

I went down the path.

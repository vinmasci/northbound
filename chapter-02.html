Northbound

Chapter Two

The Notebook

The village stood at the foot of a long hill of bracken and we came down to it in the early afternoon. There were perhaps thirty houses set in two rows along a single street, with a post office at one end and a church at the other and a small bridge over a beck running between them. The bridge was old and made of grey stone and on it a sheep was lying in the sun. It did not move as we approached. The dog stopped and looked at it and the sheep raised its head and looked back, and they regarded each other for a while in the manner of two creatures who have come to an understanding without having spoken of it. Then we walked past it onto the village street.

There were no people. There had not been people in any village I had passed through for some long time, but I had not yet stopped noticing the absence, and I do not believe I ever will. It is a thing the eye keeps looking for. I went down the street with the dog at my heel and I looked into the gardens and the windows in the way you look into the rooms of a house you have entered without knowing whose it is.

The gardens had begun to take themselves back. Roses had thrown long unpruned canes over the low walls. Apples had fallen in the small orchards and lay rotting in the grass and the wasps were drunk on them. A child's bicycle lay on its side in a driveway. I stood beside it for a while and did not touch it. The dog sniffed at one of the wheels and moved on.

I had learned by then that one ought not to enter every house. There were too many to enter and the act became a kind of grief and the grief was useless. I chose by feeling. There was a cottage near the middle of the street, painted a pale blue, with a door standing slightly ajar, and a stone trough outside it that still held an inch of green water from the last rain. I went up the path. The dog sat on the step.

The door opened easily. The hall inside was dim and smelled of old wood and a faint mustiness that was not yet decay. There was a coat on a hook. There was a pair of small green wellingtons set neatly side by side. I stood in the hallway for a long moment and listened, although I knew there would be nothing to hear, and there was nothing to hear.

The kitchen was at the back. The table was laid for one. A bowl, a spoon, a mug. There was an apple on a plate beside the bowl and the apple had wrinkled and gone brown but it had not turned black. The kettle was on the hob. I touched it with the back of my hand and it was cold of course and had been cold for I could not say how long. Beside the kettle a small notebook lay open, and a pencil with a sharp point was resting in the crease.

I went and stood over it. The handwriting was a woman's, neat and slightly slanted, in pencil that had not faded. The page held only one line. It read:

We were given the chance and most of us took it.

I read it three times. I turned the page. The next page was blank. The next, and the next. I went back to the first written page and looked at the pencil and at the bowl and the spoon, and I stood in the small clean kitchen with my hand resting flat on the wood of the table, and I tried to understand what I had been given to read.

The dog had come in behind me. He was sitting in the doorway of the kitchen looking at me with his ears forward. I do not know what he saw in my face. I closed the notebook and laid the pencil beside it as I had found it. I did not take the notebook. It seemed to me that the notebook belonged where it was, on the table, beside the apple and the bowl and the spoon, in the kitchen of a woman who had written one true sentence and gone out the door.

· · ·

Outside in the garden there was a vegetable plot that had run wild. The carrots had bolted into tall lacy flowers. The beans had thrown themselves over the canes and were dragging them down. Brambles had come over the back wall in green ropes. I picked a few late beans and put them in my pocket. The dog stood in the long grass and watched a butterfly.

I sat for a while on the back step with the door open behind me. The sun came down through the apple tree and made small moving pools of light on the flagstones. I thought about the sentence in the notebook. I thought about who the woman had been, what work she had done, whether she had lived alone or with someone she had loved, what she had heard or known that I had not, why she had thought that one line worth setting down before she went. I thought about the pencil with its careful point, and how a person who is going somewhere they do not intend to come back from will often sharpen a pencil first, although they do not know they are doing it, because the small acts hold the larger ones at bay until one is ready.

We were given the chance and most of us took it.

I had no framework in which to put the sentence. I had carried, since the beginning of my walking, a number of stories about what had happened. They were all of them stories I had made up myself, because no one had ever told me anything, and because I had stopped looking for anyone to tell me. I had thought it might have been a sickness, although there had been no bodies. I had thought it might have been a war, although there were no ruins. I had thought it might have been some failure of the chemistry of the world, although the apples still came and the wheat had set its seed in the empty fields. None of my stories had been very good and I had stopped telling them to myself.

Now there was a sentence. It did not fit any of my stories. It opened a door in a wall I had not known was a wall.

I sat on the step a long time. The dog came and lay against my hip with his chin across my thigh. I put my hand on the warm dome of his head. I do not know how long we sat there. The sun moved a hand's breadth across the flagstones.

· · ·

I did not stay in the village. I do not stay in places that have been left in the middle of a sentence. I rose at last and we went out by the front gate and back onto the street, and we walked the length of it to the church at the end. I had thought I might look in. The door was locked. I tried the handle twice and stepped back and looked up at the small grey tower with its single bell, and I thought: someone locked this. Someone went out and turned the key in the lock and put the key in their pocket. The smallness of it pleased me, in a way I cannot quite put words to. It was an act of care. It said: I am leaving, but the door behind me is closed.

I walked on past the church and out of the village, and the road climbed again into bracken and the bracken gave way to open fell. The dog ran ahead and circled back and ran ahead again. The sun was warm on my shoulders. I had a sentence in my pocket, where one had not been before.

I did not know what to do with it yet. But I had a great patience for things that did not yet know what to do with themselves, having been one of them for many years. I put one foot in front of the other and the dog went with me, and behind us the village settled back into its long quiet afternoon, and the apple in the bowl on the table in the kitchen with the door ajar held its place in the changed and continuing world.

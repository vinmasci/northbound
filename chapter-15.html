Northbound

Chapter Fifteen

Leaving

She wrote the way down on the back of an envelope at the kitchen table, in her quick neat hand, with a stub of pencil she had brought from a drawer. She wrote in two columns. On the left she wrote the names of places. On the right she wrote a single thing about each, a thing that she thought I should know that no map would tell me.

Stranraer — the road inland goes east. Take the second.

New Luce — the burn rises. Cross higher up. The sheep know the place.

Glentrool — the old keeper's bothy is dry. The door sticks at the top. Lift the handle.

Loch Doon — do not camp in the empty village. Go on past it.

Dalmellington — there is a man at the bridge if he is still there. He will let you pass. Do not go round.

And so on, for two pages, in her small fast hand, names I had heard of and names I had not. She did not lift her head much as she wrote. She wrote as though the route were a thing she was reading off a wall in front of her, and she had only to copy it. She gave me, in the end, almost two weeks of walking on the back of the envelope. She had been to none of these places, that she had said. The list was a list she had been carrying.

"How do you have this," I said, when she had finished and pushed the envelope across the table to me.

"It came to me," she said. "Bit by bit. Over the years. I have not seen the loch. I have not seen the island. But the way to it is in me. I think you understand by now."

"The list."

"Yes."

"The ones who refused."

"Yes."

I folded the envelope and put it inside the cover of the small green book and put the book in my pack. I had a strange feeling, putting it in. I had a feeling I had been given a thing that was older than the writing on it, and that the writing had only made visible a route that had already been walked, perhaps many times, by people I would never know, and that I was now to walk it because it was my turn, and the route had become, for the time being, mine. I could not have said how I had this feeling. I had it.

"Take the knife," she said.

She had wrapped it in a strip of oiled cloth, and tied it with a length of wool. She set it in front of me on the table. I unwrapped a corner. The blade was perhaps four inches, single-edged, with a short bone handle worn dark and smooth by hands. It was as plain as a knife can be. There was a maker's mark on the blade, a small fish, very faint. I drew my thumb very lightly along the edge. It was as sharp as she had said.

"It cuts bread," she said. "It cuts cheese. It cuts rope. It will skin a hare in five minutes if a hare is offered. Try not to use it on people. It is not for that, although it would do."

"Thank you," I said.

"It is not from me," she said. "It is from her. I have only kept it for someone walking."

I wrapped the knife and put it in the side pocket of the pack where I could reach it.

She came to the gate with me. She did not come down the track. She would not. She had been clear about that. She put her hand on the gatepost and stood there in the morning sun in her blue skirt with the wind in her short hair, and the dog sat between us in the gateway and looked from her to me and back to her, and after a moment he rose and went and pressed his head against the side of her thigh, and she put her hand down and rested it on his head for a long moment, and then she crouched and put her face down against his coat and stayed there.

"He likes you," I said.

"He is a good dog," she said into his coat. "He is the best of you. Look after him. He is a long way from home, this one."

"He has never had a home that I know of."

"No," she said. She straightened up. "But he will know it when he comes to it. Dogs know."

I looked at her. I looked at the cottage behind her, white in the late morning sun, and the rose bushes at the wall, and the line of washing she had hung the day I had come, which she would take down today or tomorrow.

"Iona."

"Yes."

"I would have stayed."

"I know," she said. "And we would have made a small soft life together, you and I, and the dog, and the firth, and the rocks in the morning. And it would have been a good life, for some years. I have thought about it. I have thought about it on and off since the second night. It would have been good."

"But."

"But you are walking toward a thing. And the thing is not me. I would have known it the whole while. I would have watched you sit at our table and look out at our firth and be a kind, attentive man, and I would have watched you wonder, every morning, what the cottage on the island looked like at first light. I would have watched you not knowing whether the deer still came down to drink. I would have watched it for years. I would not have minded for myself. I would have minded for you. You would have grown small. I do not want you to grow small. You are not a man for it."

"You think you know me."

"I think I know the man you would become if you stopped now," she said. "Yes. I think I know him very well. I have known versions of him before."

I did not say anything. There was nothing useful to say.

She came round the gatepost. She put her arms round me. She held me for perhaps a minute, with her cheek against my collar, and her hands flat on my back. She did not speak. She stepped back. She took my face in her hands and looked at me. She kissed me once on the mouth, gently, with closed eyes. She let go. She stepped back.

"Go on," she said. "Mind the road at New Luce. Do not stop in the empty village. Eat the apples in the order I gave you. The small ones first."

"All right."

"And when you find quiet," she said, "sit in it. That is the only advice I have for you that no one else has given. Sit in it. Do not move on through it. Most people, when they find quiet, walk through it as though it were a corridor. It is not a corridor. It is a room. Sit in it."

"All right."

"Go," she said.

I went. The dog hesitated at the gate. He looked back at her once. She nodded to him and he turned and trotted after me with his head down, and he did not look back again. When I had gone two hundred yards down the track and turned at the bend by the wood I looked back myself. She was still at the gate. She lifted a hand once. I lifted mine. She turned and went back into the garden. I could see her bend to take a sheet from the line.

I went on down the track.

· · ·

I walked thirty miles in two days after I left her. I do not know whether this was because I was in a hurry to be far enough away that I could not turn back, or because she had given me, in her own particular way, a kind of charge that ran in my legs and would not let me sit. The dog kept up without complaint. He did not look at me much in those two days. I think he understood that I needed not to be looked at. I think he had agreed with her, on his own account, that this was the right thing.

I walked along the coast and the coast was long and beautiful and the sea birds came and went and at one point a pod of porpoises moved along parallel to the shore for an hour, rolling slowly out of the water and back into it, and I watched them as I walked and the dog watched them too, and we did not speak of them. I had been able, in those two days, to read the country in a way I had not been able to before. I do not know how to describe this. The shape of a hill. The sound of a bird. The way a stand of birches stood a particular way against a sky. I had begun to read these things as one reads a sentence in a language one is just beginning to be able to read, with effort, but with the start of understanding. I had Iona to thank for this, although I could not have said how. The hour on the rocks, perhaps. The afternoons in the chair by the window. Her hand on the back of my hand on the bench. Some part of me had been, briefly, slowed enough to see, and although I was now walking again at the pace of a man with a long road still ahead, the seeing had not gone. I could carry it. It was light. It weighed less than a small green book in a pack.

I sat that night by a fire in a place above a beach that she had mentioned, although she had not said why. There was a small spring there and a grove of low wind-shaped pines and a flat patch of grass for a tent. I laid out the cloak I had been carrying as a groundsheet and the dog lay on it and I sat by the fire and I ate one of the apples she had given me, the smallest first, as she had said. The apple was sharp and good. I ate it down to the core. I gave the core to the dog who chewed it carefully, the way he chewed everything that mattered. I put my back against a stone that had been warmed by the day's sun. I looked at the fire.

When you find quiet, she had said, sit in it. I sat in it. The fire crackled small. The sea below moved against the shingle in its long slow voice. The dog breathed. I sat in the quiet for what felt like a great while, and was perhaps not so long, and the quiet, in the way of such things, made a place inside me where it could be returned to later. I would return to it later. I had been given that as well.

I lay down in time and the dog moved closer along my hip. The stars came out as they had done every night now for many nights, in their old patient houses, and I slept, and dreamed, although the dream did not stay with me into the morning.

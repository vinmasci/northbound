Northbound

Chapter Eighteen

The Trossachs

I came to Loch Lomond at evening, by a track that dropped down through a wood of oak and birch and ended on a strip of stony shore where small waves were running in from the open water. The loch lay long and dark before me, the hills standing on either side of it black against a sky that had begun to colour. I stopped on the shore and stood there for some long time. The dog went down to the water and stood with his front paws in it and drank carefully, lifting his head between drinks to look out at the loch as though he were drinking to it as much as from it.

The size of it took me. I had been told it was a long water. I had been told it was a deep one. I had not been told what it was to come down out of a wood and see its full length lying ahead of one in the failing light, with the blue hills rising into the cloud at the far end and the small islands of darker green set in it, and not a single small light anywhere on its shores, and not a single small wake on its surface, and a hundred thousand years of weather in the look of it. I was a small man on a stone beach. The loch did not need me. The loch had not needed any of us. The loch had been here when there had been Picts on its shores and it had been here when there had been Romans in the plains below, and it had been here when there had been monks in cells on the islands praying their prayers, and it had been here when a steamer had run up and down the water taking people from one village to another, and now there was no steamer, and no village had a person in it, and the loch was the loch as it had always been, and was perhaps a little gladder now of the quiet, although I could not say that for sure.

I walked north along the shore. I had ten or twelve miles in me before dark, perhaps. The track ran along under the eastern flank of the loch, sometimes by the water and sometimes climbing into the wood above. I went on. The dog ran ahead. He had put the limp behind him, although I watched him for it still and would for some long while.

I camped that night in a clearing under an oak above the shore, with a small fire of fallen branches that I had to break with my heel because the wood was too sodden to snap. I cooked porridge. I gave the dog the last of the cheese in small pieces and he took each one with his old polite gravity. The fire smoked more than it burned. I lay down at last under the oak with the dog along my back, and I listened to the loch making its small soft sound on the stones below us, and I thought about how I had only six days now, by Iona's reckoning, until I would be on the shore of the sea-loch where my grandmother's island stood. Six days had become a small unit. It was nothing now. It was a scrap of time. I had walked many such scraps without counting and I would walk this one. I closed my eyes. Sleep came easily.

· · ·

The country I went into the next day was the country of the songs. I had not known the songs. I had known fragments. I had heard my grandmother humming one of them at her stove, a tune called Mist Covered Mountains, the same air Walenty had played on his fiddle in his kitchen by the range, although I had not known then that the two were the same. I had been in the building of glass when she had sung. I had been a long way from any mist or any mountain. I had loved her, in my small distracted way, and I had not asked her about the song. I had asked her almost nothing. Later, when she had died, I had wished I had asked her many things. I had not, at that time, gone to her funeral. I had been on a flight to a city in another country to attend a meeting that I could not have described under questioning a year afterwards. The flight had cost the building of glass perhaps two thousand pounds and the meeting had decided, in the end, nothing.

I thought about my grandmother as I walked up the eastern shore of Loch Lomond on a morning when the sun came down through the oak woods in long shafts of green and gold. I had not let myself think about her for a long time. There had been, after her funeral, a small cold place in me where she had been, and I had walked round it for some years, and I had eventually built over it the way a man builds a shed over a difficult patch of his garden. I had not been into the shed in many years. I had not even known it was there. The walking, the long quiet walking, had taken the shed apart board by board without my consent. I had begun, somewhere south of Loch Lomond, to be inside the place where she had been. I was inside it now. It was not a cold place. It had never been a cold place. I had been calling it cold because I had not been able to face it. It was a place full of small warm things. Her hands. Her stove. A pair of her shoes by a door. A song.

I walked up the loch and I thought about her. I gave her the time I had not given her. It was a poor offering, twenty years late, but the dead do not, I came to believe that morning, count the lateness. They take what is offered and they ask no questions of it. They are kinder than the living in that respect. The living count.

By noon I had wept once, briefly, walking, without breaking stride. The dog had looked back at me but had not stopped. He seemed to understand that the weeping was a small business and would conclude itself if not attended to. He was right. It concluded. I went on.

· · ·

That afternoon I came up over a low pass in the hills and into the country called the Trossachs. I had heard the name before. I had not known what country it named. It named a country of small lochs set in the folds of low rough mountains, and woods of birch and rowan running up the slopes, and wide stretches of bracken and heather glowing in the afternoon, and high above all of these a sky that was now beginning to be a real autumn sky, with banks of grey cloud moving from the west and patches of clear blue between them, and the light coming and going in slow swaths across the country.

The track took me along the shoulder of a hill above one of the small lochs. There was no road in this country, not anymore, only the old paths the country had taught itself again. I came round a bend in the track and ahead of me a stag was standing in the path. He had been crossing from the wood on the right to the wood on the left and he had heard us coming and had stopped. He stood in the path with his head up and his great horn-rack against the sky and he looked at me. The dog stopped behind me. The dog did not move. He had grown wise about deer.

The stag and I held each other's eyes for a long moment. I could see his breath in the cold air. He was an old stag, I think. The rack was heavy and broad and the points were many. He had a long working scar down one shoulder, perhaps from a fall, perhaps from another stag, perhaps from a long-ago fence. He looked at me the way a creature looks at another creature when it is deciding what the other creature is. I had not had a creature look at me that way in any of my walking, except perhaps the bee woman's bees, and that had been a different looking. The stag looked at me as a king looks at a stranger come into his hall.

I bowed my head. I do not know why I did this. It seemed to me at the time to be the right thing. I bowed it slightly and held it bowed for a moment and then lifted my eyes again. The stag was still there. He had not moved. He looked at me a moment longer, and then he tossed his head once, an almost dismissive gesture, and walked across the track unhurried, and went into the wood on the left, and was gone.

I stood in the path for a while afterwards. The dog came up to my side and stood there. I think we both, the dog and I, understood that we had been given a small audience and that the audience had concluded with our being permitted to walk on. I would think of the stag for some long time after. I think of him still. He is in this country, I imagine, even now, as I write this, doing whatever a stag does in the long autumn evenings of a country emptied of men. He is somewhere in those Trossachs. He has perhaps fallen by now, since stags do fall, and another, younger stag is in his country. But the line of him goes on. The country knows him. The wood knows him. He is the kind of thing the country, after the men have gone, returns into being. There is a great deal to be said for a country that returns into stags.

· · ·

I crossed the Trossachs in two days. The country went on giving me its small audiences. A pair of red squirrels in a birch beside the path who held still for me as I passed, going about their work as soon as I had gone. An otter on the bank of a burn at dusk, sliding into the water as the dog and I came up, not so much in fear as in a shifting from one room of its house to another. A skein of geese coming over high one cold morning, very high, so high I had to stand still and crane my neck back to see them, and they went over from north-east to south-west in their long slow pulse, calling to each other, and the calls came down through the cold air like very small bells.

I had been a man who had not seen a goose in nineteen years.

I came down out of the Trossachs on the third morning into a wide green strath where a river ran north, and I knew, looking at the river going north between its low banks, that I was now in the last country of my walking. There were perhaps three days more. The hills ahead were higher and bluer and the names on Iona's envelope had begun to be names from the songs I had begun to hear my grandmother humming again. The Black Mount. Glencoe. Loch Etive. Beyond Loch Etive there was the sea-loch and the island. The dog walked in front of me with his easy long stride. I had a feeling in my chest I did not have a name for. It was not fear. It was not joy. It was something more nearly like the way one feels at the end of a long letter one has been writing all night, when the last paragraph is at hand and one is turning the words over to find which ones will be the last ones. I would not know which ones until I came to them.

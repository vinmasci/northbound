Northbound

Chapter Seventeen

The City

Iona had told me to go round Glasgow. She had not said why. She had said only, Do not go through. Skirt it on the south. There is a hill above Eaglesham where you can see it. Stop there. Look. Go on. I had written it down on the envelope. I had not asked for more.

I came up out of the south country on a long October morning, with the haze of autumn beginning to take the trees, and the road, when it climbed the last shoulder of hill above Eaglesham, gave me the city.

I stopped where she had said to stop. There was a wide grass verge at the top of the rise and a stone bench that someone had put there in the long ago, perhaps a Victorian, perhaps a more recent council. I sat on the stone bench. The dog sat at my feet. The city lay below us.

It lay along the great low river in its broad pale plain, the buildings going on for miles, the spires and the cranes and the long ranks of tenements with their grey slate roofs, the warehouse rooves of the south side, the parks lying among them as small dark green pieces, the great glass office blocks at the centre catching the sun. It was beautiful. I had not expected this. I had walked toward it expecting to be made sad by it, the way ruins make a person sad. It was not sad. It was very still. The river was a long curving silver in the morning light. The sky above it was crossed by no smoke, no aircraft, no lines of any kind. There were birds. There were birds in such numbers I could see them, even at the distance, in slow turning columns above the parks. They had taken the city in their soft slow way and made of it a roosting ground. The sky above Glasgow was the most populated part of Glasgow.

I sat for a long time on the stone bench.

· · ·

I had lived in such a city. I had not lived in this city. I had lived in another, further south, but the difference between the two was small, the way the difference between two great rivers is small to a man who has never been on either of them. I had taken trains in such a city and I had crossed bridges in such a city and I had stood, on certain mornings, on certain corners, waiting for certain lights to change. I had bought coffee in paper cups and walked with the cup in my hand and not tasted the coffee. I had had a coat for such a city. I had had shoes. I had had a face for such a city, which I had put on each morning before I left the flat and which I had taken off, finally, in the lift on the way up to the office, although I had not known I had been taking it off. I had become, in such a city, a man who walked very fast on flat surfaces. I had not noticed how fast.

The city below me was the same city, mostly. The shapes of the buildings were the shapes of the buildings I had known. The ranks of windows. The patterns of the streets. The shapes of the bridges over the river. They were the inheritance of a particular age, my age, the age that had built such places. They were the shapes that the building of glass had made.

It was very quiet. It was so quiet that, looking down at it from the bench, I could begin to hear the absence of the sound it had made when it had been working. I could hear, in the great silence, the shape of the great noise. The noise had been continuous and vast and I had taken it for the natural sound of the world for nineteen years. The noise had been engines. The noise had been ventilation. The noise had been the small low constant note that buildings of glass make, a hum of cooling, a hum of lifting, a hum of a thousand servers stacked in basements doing their work behind walls. The noise had been the low rumble of the underground beneath the streets. The noise had been the dop-dop-dop of the helicopters that policed it from above on busy nights. The noise had been the human voice, in its tens of thousands, making itself heard against the noise. The noise had been against itself. The noise had been the city at war with the noise of the city.

The noise was gone. The buildings stood. The river ran. The birds came and went above the parks in their slow soft columns.

I sat on the bench and I looked at the city and I felt for it a thing I had never felt for it when I had lived in it. I felt tenderness. I felt something close to love. I had never loved the city. I had endured it. It had endured me. We had been, I and the city, two parties in a long contract neither of which had been signed. Now the contract was over. The city was free. It stood there in the bright morning above its slow silver river, with no city left in it but only its bones, and the bones were beautiful, and the birds above the parks knew it, although I had not known it before.

The dog leaned against my knee. He looked at the city. He yawned. He laid his head on his paws.

· · ·

I had loved one woman in the city. She had not been the woman I lived with for the four years before the building of glass began to empty. She had been earlier, in the second year of my work. She had been a girl with a small flat in the east, and a dog, and a particular way of looking at me from the corner of her eye, and she had loved me, I think, more than I had loved her, which is a thing one does not quite believe at the time and which one does not stop being ashamed of later. We had been together for nine months. I had broken it off because I had been about to be promoted in the building of glass and the promotion had felt to me, at the time, more important than the girl. I had used a phrase I will not put down here. She had not cried. She had looked at me from the corner of her eye for a moment and had said, "You will know, one day, what you have just done. I am sorry it will be that day, when you know." And she had gone out of the café and I had not seen her again.

I sat on the bench above Eaglesham and I let her face come up out of the city. I had not let it come up in a long time. It came up now without permission, the way faces come up when one is tired and is sitting still. She had had a small mark on the side of her chin from a fall when she was a child. She had had pale eyes. She had laughed easily, at small things, in the way some people do who are paying close attention to the world. She had been called Mary. I had not used her name in nineteen years.

"Mary," I said now, on the bench, to no one. The dog turned his head and looked at me. The name went out and went down off the hill toward the city in the air, and the city, in its great quiet, did not give it back. She was somewhere in there or she was not. She was perhaps in a field somewhere, with all the others who had walked out into fields. She was perhaps not. She had perhaps been one of the refusers, like the bee woman, like Iona, and was on a hillside in the islands tending some quiet work of her own. I would not know. I would not see her again. I sat on the bench and I let myself be sorry for what I had done at the table in the café in the second year of my work, and I let the being sorry have its own time, and not be hurried, the way Iona had taught me to let things have their own time. The dog did not interrupt. After a while the being sorry was finished. It had been a small thing in the end. I was sorry. I had done a wrong thing. The wrong thing had had consequences I could not now reach. I was sorry. The wrong thing did not require more of me than the being sorry. It required, perhaps, that I do better with the next person who looked at me from the corner of her eye, although there might not now be a next person, and the wrong thing did not lessen for that, and was not greater for it either.

I rose from the bench. The dog rose. We walked on north along the road that ran above the city, and the city dropped away on our right hand, and I did not look back at it for a long time.

· · ·

That afternoon a peregrine took a pigeon out of the air above a wood we were passing. There was a sudden falling stoop, very fast, almost too fast to see, and then the small explosion of feathers in the air, and the peregrine going off with the pigeon hanging from its talons. It happened in perhaps three seconds. The dog and I stood and watched it. The wood went on rustling in the small wind. A few of the pigeon's feathers came down very slowly through the trees.

I had never seen a peregrine in my old life. I had perhaps seen one and not known. I would not now mistake it. I had a great hunger, in those days, to learn the names of the things that were doing their work around me, the names of the trees and the names of the birds and the names of the small flowers in the verges, and I had begun to ask the dog the names, although he did not know them, in the way one asks a creature one is walking with about the country one is walking through, more for the asking than for the answer.

"Peregrine," I said to him.

He looked at me. He looked at the wood. He understood that the matter was concluded, the bird gone, the feathers down, and he walked on, and I walked after him.

The road bent east and then north and the country opened out into a wide plain with low blue mountains beginning to rise on the far side of it. Loch Lomond was somewhere ahead. I had never been to Loch Lomond. I had heard the song. I had imagined it as a small thing, a stretch of water in a postcard. It was, I would shortly find, a great long water under great blue hills, the entrance to a country I would not have known how to imagine. The road was taking me toward it. I was going to walk into the songs at last. I had not known, in the building of glass, that the songs had been the truth.

Northbound

Chapter Thirteen

Iona

I left Walenty on a Wednesday, with a parcel of bread and cheese in my pack and a small jar of honey he had taken from a comb in his pantry, and a pat of yellow butter wrapped in a wet cloth which he said would last me a day if I was careful. He came as far as the gate. He did not embrace me. He laid his hand on my shoulder and said something in Polish I did not catch, and then he turned and went back up the yard with the two collies at his heel. The collies looked once over their shoulders at the dog and the dog looked once at them, and that was the whole farewell among them. The dog was good at such things. I had perhaps still some learning to do.

The road took me west. I had begun to angle west across the country toward the coast because the country bent that way and the road bent with it, and because, in some part of myself I could not give an account of, I had begun to want the sea. I had not seen the sea since some time before the building of glass had emptied of its workers, and I had not seen it then with eyes that were paying attention. I wanted the sea now. I wanted to walk along the edge of a great water for a time before I went up into the mountains.

I came down to the Solway on the third day. The road dropped through a wood of birch and rowan and at the bottom of the wood it opened out and there was the great long firth lying flat and silver in the afternoon sun, with the Cumberland fells very small and dim on the far shore, and the mudflats running out to it in long wet stripes left by the tide. I stopped in the road and looked at it. The dog stopped beside me. He had not seen the sea before, that I knew of. He sat down. He looked at it for a long time. Then he turned and looked at me, as though to ask whether the sea was something we walked into or something we walked beside, and I said, "We walk beside it," and he seemed satisfied, and we went on.

· · ·

The house stood by itself at the end of a track that turned off the coast road and ran along the top of a low cliff above the shore. I had walked along the coast road for an hour before I came to the track, and I had been thinking that it was getting late and that I should soon find a place to camp, and I had turned up the track only because there had been a small wooden sign at its mouth with two words on it written in a careful hand. The words were Come in. There was no name. There was no arrow. There was only the small sign on its post, painted white, with the two words.

I stood at the mouth of the track and looked at the sign for some long time. The dog sat in the road and watched me decide. I had read it three times before I started up the track.

The house was a long low whitewashed cottage with a slate roof, set back from the cliff edge in a small walled garden of grass and rose-bushes that had run halfway to wild. There was a chimney. There was no smoke. The door was painted a deep dark blue. The door was open.

I stopped at the gate. I called out, although I could not have said what I called out. Some sound that was neither a word nor a name. The dog stood at my heel.

A woman came round the side of the house. She had been hanging washing on a line at the back. She held a wooden peg in one hand. She was perhaps thirty-five, with dark hair cut short, and she wore a man's shirt and a long blue skirt and her feet were bare on the grass. She stopped at the corner of the house and looked at me. Her face was very still. The stillness was the first thing I noticed about her. The bee woman had been still in the way of an old bird on a fence post that has been considering the world for a long time. Edmund had been still in the way of a man who has set a stone down. This woman was still in a different way. Her stillness had the quality of water in a deep bowl, the surface of it flat, the shape of it given by the bowl, the depth of it not visible from above.

She looked at the dog. The dog walked through the open gate into the garden and went up to her and sat down at her bare feet without looking back at me. He had not done this before. He had never gone to anyone before me. He sat at her feet and looked up at her with his patient face, and after a moment she put her hand down and rested it on the top of his head.

"You'll be wanting tea," she said. Her voice was low. There was a faint Hebridean lilt in it, an island I could not place.

"I read the sign," I said.

"I know you did," she said. "Or you would not be here."

"What is the sign for."

"For whoever needs it," she said. She looked at me very levelly. "It does not seem to be for many. You are the second this year."

· · ·

Her name was Iona. The house had been her grandmother's. She had been a teacher, in Glasgow, of small children, and she had come south to the cottage for a holiday in a particular August and had not gone back. She told me this in the kitchen while she made tea, in the low quick way one mentions the weather, as though the not going back were a fact of the world like a hill or a river and not a story to be told. The kettle on the stove was a copper kettle. The tea was strong. The cups were thick stoneware, white-glazed inside, the clay showing brown at the rim where the glaze had worn.

"Why did you not go back," I said.

"It came," she said. "I had been here for a week when it came. I had the windows open. It came in through the windows. I sat at this table. I drank a cup of tea. It came in and it sat with me. I had not been afraid. I had thought I would be. I had read about such things. I had thought, if such a thing came, I would be afraid. I was not. It sat with me as though it were known to me. As though we had met, and only forgotten."

"And then."

"Then it asked me," she said. "And I said no. And it went."

"Just like that."

"Just like that. I was perhaps lucky. Others I have heard of, they had longer with it. They had to argue. I did not have to argue. I think it knew. There is something in me that it knew at once would say no. Other people have it, that thing. Walenty has it. The bee woman in Hawes, if you came that way, has it."

I looked up at her. "You know them."

"I know of them," she said. "We are not many, the ones who refused. We hear of each other, in time. There is a way of hearing, when you have refused. The thing leaves a small mark on you. The mark is a kind of open door. You hear of others through it. You do not seek them. They reach you."

"Is that how you knew about the second person this year. Before me."

"Yes," she said. "And it is how I knew you were coming, this morning. I did not know it was you. I knew someone was on the road. I had taken the eggs out at first light and I had stood by the gate and I had felt it. I had thought, then, perhaps in three days. You are quicker than I thought. You are walking strong."

I sat with my hand on the cup and looked at her across the table. I had not spoken to anyone of my own knowledge of the thing. I had been carrying it close. It did not entirely surprise me to find that there was a way of hearing among the refusers, although I had not known of it before. It seemed to me that the world had been re-organising itself, in the long quiet, into shapes I was only now beginning to learn the outlines of. The bee woman had had her bees. Edmund had had his garden and his cat. The cataloguer had had his ledger. Walenty had had his cows. Iona, here in this cottage on the cliff, had perhaps had a way of standing by a gate at first light and feeling who was on the road. Each of them had a small work of their own, and the works had begun, in some way I could not see, to know each other.

"Why the sign," I said.

"Because some who walk are walking toward something and some are walking away," she said, "and the ones walking away need a place to stop, in case the walking has not yet been the cure. I cannot tell which is which from the gate. So I put the sign. Whoever needs it, comes in. Whoever does not, walks past, and does not see it, perhaps. The track does not lead anywhere. There is only the sea."

"I am walking toward something," I said.

"I know you are," she said. "I knew when you came up the track. Your dog walks like a dog whose man is walking toward something. They tell you, dogs."

"Then why did you let me in."

"You did not need a place to stop," she said. "But you needed a meal and a bed for a night or two. There is no harm in that. The sign said come in, and you came in, and so you may have what the house offers, which is a meal and a bed for a night or two. After that you will go on to whatever you are walking toward. You will not stay. People who are walking toward do not stay. I would not wish you to."

· · ·

That evening we walked the shore together. The tide was a long way out and the mudflats stretched away from us toward the silver line of the firth, and the light was the long gold light of late summer that holds and holds, and the dog ran out onto the flats and came back muddy and ran out again. Iona had put on a pair of old wellingtons and rolled the long blue skirt up and tied a knot in it at her thigh, and we walked side by side along the firm strip of sand at the foot of the cliff, our shadows going ahead of us, and we did not speak.

I had been a man, in the building of glass, who had filled silences. I had thought it a kindness. I had thought a silence was a thing one ought to ease for another person. I had not understood until I walked beside Iona that hour that a silence between two people who are both willing to be in it is a thing of its own, and is not waiting to be eased, and is in fact harmed by easing, the way a still surface is harmed by stones. We walked. The tide turned somewhere far out and began its long slow coming back. A curlew called. The dog came up dripping and shook himself between us, and we both laughed, and then we did not speak again, and the silence resumed, and was deeper.

When we came up the path to the cottage at dusk she paused at the gate.

"Stay tonight," she said. "Stay tomorrow if you like. There is a small room at the end. Eggs in the morning. I would not say longer."

"That is what Walenty said."

"Yes," she said. "It is what we say. We learned it from each other, perhaps. It is the right thing."

"Why."

"Because if you stay longer," she said, "you will stop walking. And you have not yet finished walking. I do not want to be the place you stopped walking. I want to be the place you rested for a night or two, and then went on, and arrived where you were going. That is a better thing for me to be."

I looked at her in the dim light at the gate. The dog was at her feet again. He had taken to her completely. He had spent the whole of our walk along the shore checking back at her almost more than at me, and she had not seemed to notice or to mind.

"I will stay tonight," I said. "I will stay tomorrow. Then I will go on."

"Yes," she said. "That is right."

And we went into the cottage, and she lit the lamp on the table, and she made supper, and we ate it, and the dog lay on the mat by the door, and outside the firth came back in its slow long coming, and the night was the kind of night I had not known I had been missing.

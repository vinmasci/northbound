Northbound

Chapter Twenty-Five

The Deer

Autumn came over the island slowly. The bracken on the hill went from green to a deep bronze and then to a slow rust, and the grasses on the headlands above the bay went pale, and the sea took on the dark heavy colour it takes in the last weeks of the year, and the wind from the west carried the cold of the open ocean into every corner of the cottage, and I learned, day by day, the small work it took to live on a small island in such a season.

I cut peat from the cut by the south wall, as the list in the box had told me. I left the new face. I split logs from the small pile of driftwood Donal had brought across in the spring of that year and stacked under the lean-to at the side of the cottage. I baked bread on Tuesdays and Fridays, badly at first, and then less badly, in the small black oven at the side of the range. I caught fish off the rocks at the south end of the bay with a hand line and a hook of Iona's blade-trimmed bone. I dug what was left in the small kitchen garden behind the cottage and found two rows of late potatoes that had survived without minding, and I ate them with the fish, and gave the dog his share. He grew his winter coat, slowly. He thickened. He took to lying on the warm flags by the range in the long evenings, and to coming up the stair behind me at bedtime, and to sleeping at the foot of the bed.

I did not, in those weeks, walk south to the other end of the island. I did not see Donal's wife. I did not, at that time, want to. I had a feeling I ought to be alone with the cottage for a while. I had a feeling that the meeting with her would come when it was right, and that the rightness was not yet, and that she, who had lived on the island for forty-one years, would understand. I think she did. I think she had been the same way when she had come, alone, and she had let the place teach her in its quiet patient way before she had let any human voice into the teaching. I would meet her in the spring. I had decided this. The decision did not weigh on me. There was time.

I read in the evenings. I had opened the second drawer of the chest, after some weeks, and I had found in it the books my grandmother had left me. There were not many. There were a dozen, perhaps. They were the kind of books one reads slowly. One was the small green book I had been carrying since the cataloguer had given it to me, although in a different edition. It made me smile to find it there. The cataloguer had given me a copy of a book my grandmother had owned. The book had been waiting for me at both ends of the road. I do not know how to account for this. I do not try.

· · ·

Donal came across once before the loch shut for the winter. He brought flour and salt and a sack of seed for the spring. He brought a small wooden box with a few candles in it. He brought news, which was that there was none. A scrap of Iona's handwriting had reached him by one of the slow routes the keepers used, and he had been pleased to hear that I had arrived safely and was well, although he had not doubted. He sat in the kitchen with me for a half-hour. He drank tea. He ate a piece of my bread, which I had baked that morning. He said, "Better than the first one would have been, I expect." I told him it was. He nodded.

"The tide is good tomorrow," he said.

I looked at him.

"For what."

"For leaving," he said. "If leaving is what you want."

The dog lifted his head from the flags. Donal did not look at him. He looked at me, and there was nothing in his face that pushed me one way or the other.

"And if I do not leave tomorrow."

"Then you do not leave tomorrow."

"Would you come again."

"When the loch allows. In spring, perhaps. Perhaps before if the winter is kind, but I would not build a choice on a kind winter."

I went to the window. The bay was grey and full. The rain had stopped but the glass still held beads of water along the lower edge of each pane. Somewhere beyond the hill, beyond the other end of the island, his wife was in her own cottage among her stones and bones and small painted looks. Somewhere beyond the loch the road I had walked went back through every house that had offered me a bed, every field where shoes had been set by a gate, every name tied under a stone. Somewhere, if the letter was right, my mother had gone on walking toward my father, and perhaps had reached him, or perhaps was still on some long road made entirely of wanting.

"I do not know," I said.

"Good," Donal said.

I turned from the window.

"Good?"

"A man who knows too quickly is usually being carried."

He finished his tea. He set the cup down. "I will sleep on the boat tonight. At first light I will be at the jetty. If you are there, I will take you back. If you are not there, I will go home. I will not come up to fetch you. That is not my work."

He did not stay long in the cottage. He wanted the boat settled before dark. I walked down with him to the small jetty in the inlet where I had landed in the summer. The day was cold and bright. The water in the inlet was clear and deep and a few small fish moved over the white sand of the bottom. He climbed into the boat. He stowed the empty bag he had brought my goods in. He looked up at me.

"Spring," he said.

"Or morning," I said.

"Or morning."

"My wife will come across the hill to you, perhaps, if she wants. Or not. She will choose."

"She will choose. Yes."

"Look after the dog."

"I will."

"Look after yourself."

"I will."

He cast off. He took up the oars. He pulled across the inlet in his long quiet pulls and dropped anchor in the lee of the outer rock, where the boat lay small and dark on the water. The dog stood at the head of the jetty and watched him settle the oars and duck beneath the little canvas cover he had rigged over the stern. I stood beside the dog until the light began to go out of the day and the boat became a shadow among shadows.

The dog turned his head and looked at me. He stood up. We walked back together up the path, between the low rocks at the top of the beach, across the open ground above the inlet, over the small rise from which I had first seen the cottage, and down into the green hollow where the cottage stood.

The chimney had a thin column of smoke coming straight up from it.

I did not sleep much. Before first light I rose and put on my boots and went down to the kitchen. The dog lifted his head from the flags but did not get up. I opened the door. The path to the jetty was pale in the dark. I could see the inlet below, and the boat at the rock, and Donal already awake in it, a darker shape among the dark. The tide was turning. If I walked down then, he would take me. He would ask nothing. I could go back across the loch, back to the road, back to the long work of finding where my mother had gone, or where my father waited, or where my grandmother had hidden herself in death. There would always be another north, if I wanted one badly enough.

The dog came to the door and stood beside me. He did not push past. He did not look toward the jetty. He looked into the kitchen, at the range where the fire was banked, at the table, at the bread wrapped in its cloth. Then he looked at me.

I closed the door.

I went to the range and opened the draught and put on two pieces of peat. The fire took slowly, then well. When the kettle began to speak, I opened the door again. The boat had left the rock. Donal was rowing out of the inlet in his long quiet pulls. He did not look back. I did not lift a hand. That was not because I did not love him in that moment. It was because he had made the choice clean for me, and a wave would have put some of the choice back into his keeping.

Later, when the light had come fully and the kettle had boiled and the dog had eaten, I walked up to the rise above the cottage. The dog came with me. Donal's boat was a small dark mark far out on the water, already more sea than boat. Below us, the chimney had a thin column of smoke coming straight up from it.

I stopped on the rise. I looked down. The dog stopped beside me.

I had become, I understood then, a person whose smoke went up out of a chimney. I had not been such a person before. I had been, in my old life, a person whose name appeared on a great many invoices and whose face appeared on a great many internal company directories and whose login appeared in a great many systems, and none of those appearances had been, in any way that mattered, a smoke going up out of a chimney. I had not been visible to the world as a smoke. I had not been visible to the world as anything, perhaps, in a way the world cared to see.

Now I was a smoke. The smoke was a thin one. The chimney was a small chimney. The fire was a fire of peat and driftwood, in a small range, in a small cottage, on a small island. But the smoke went up. The world, looking down, would have seen it. The world, looking down, sees smoke going up out of chimneys. It sees little else, in the matter of what people do. The smoke is the work. The work is the smoke. I had not understood this in my old life. I had thought the work was the things one wrote down and the things one was paid for and the things one's name was attached to. The work, it turns out, is the smoke. There is no other work.

I went down with the dog into the hollow.

· · ·

I sat that evening on the stone above the bay.

I had taken to sitting there at dusk, in the late afternoons, when the light was beginning to go and the wind was coming up off the water. There was a flat slab on the slope above the cottage, a few yards from the wall, with a small back-rest of rock behind it, and I had begun, in the manner perhaps of Iona on her own rocks above the firth, to go and sit there for an hour at the day's hinge. I did not do anything when I sat. I did not read. I did not write. I did not eat. I sat on the stone with the dog at my feet, and I looked out at the bay, and I attended.

I had not, until those evenings, used the word attended for what I was doing. I am not sure now whether the word is right. The bee woman had said they kept their work. The cataloguer had said the work requires only attention and hands and time. Iona had said sit in it. The dog had said nothing in human words, but had taught me, more than any of them, what the doing of the thing looked like from the outside. I sat and I attended. I did not try to attend to anything in particular. I attended to the bay. I attended to the dog beside me. I attended to my own breath going in and out. I attended to the small wind off the water. The attending was the thing. There was nothing else I had to do with the hour.

It was on one of those evenings, at the very end of October by my best count, that they came down.

I had been on the stone perhaps twenty minutes. The sun had gone behind the western islands and the sky was a long band of gold above them and a deep grey blue above that. The bay was full of the tide. There was a single seal on the rock at the far side. The dog was beside me with his head on his paws.

I felt them before I saw them. I do not know what to call the feeling. It was a small movement of attention, perhaps the dog's, perhaps mine, perhaps the world's, the way a room changes when a person comes into it from another room and one has not yet looked up. I looked toward the path that came down the hill from the north of the bay.

They were coming down. There were three of them. There was a hind in front, and behind her a smaller hind, and behind her again the great stag I had seen in the inlet on my first day. They came down the path in the slow careful way of deer, lifting and setting their feet, pausing now and again to look. The first hind was looking ahead. The second was looking back at the stag. The stag was looking at the bay. They came on down the path until they reached the shore of the small bay below my stone, perhaps a hundred yards from where I sat, and the first hind walked out onto the wet stones of the shore and lowered her head to the small fresh-water stream that ran down out of the hill into the bay at that point, and drank.

The dog did not move. He did not lift his head. He had heard them, of course, before I had felt them. He had decided, before I had even sat down on the stone perhaps, that they were not a thing to be moved at. He had been waiting for me to learn this. Perhaps he had been waiting all along.

The hind drank. Then the stag drank. Then the smaller hind drank. They lifted their heads in their own time. The great stag looked along the shore. He looked up the slope. He looked at the cottage with its thin smoke going straight up against the gold. He looked at the stone where I sat. He looked, for a moment, at me.

I bowed my head, slightly, as I had bowed it in the Trossachs.

The stag did not bow his head. He did not need to. He looked at me a moment longer, and then he turned, and the three of them walked back along the shore the way they had come, and went up the path in the long careful way they had come down, and over the rise, and were gone.

· · ·

I sat on the stone for a long while after they had gone. The light went out of the western sky. The seal slipped off its rock. The first stars began to come.

I had been a man, in the old life, who had run on a great noise. I had not heard, in the noise, the world. I had not heard my own breath. I had not heard the breath of any other creature. I had not heard the small fresh-water stream coming down a hill into a bay where deer drank in the evenings. I had not heard my grandmother humming an old air over a stove. I had not heard the small voice of any of the things that had been doing their work all around me, and I had not, finally, heard the voice that came at last, when it came, asking gently if I was tired.

I had not been spared. I know that now. I had been asked in the same way the others had been asked, only my question had taken the shape of a road. It had put a cottage at the end of it, and behind the cottage a grandmother, and behind her a mother in a green coat, and behind her a father whose face I knew from a photograph. I had walked toward them all. I had walked with love and anger and hunger and the old child's wish to be gathered up by the people who had gone before he could ask why.

And then the road had ended at a door, and the door had opened into work. Not comfort. Not answer. Work. A hinge to oil. Bread to bake. Peat to cut. A dog to feed. A fire to lay so that smoke would go up in the morning and tell the empty country that someone living had chosen the living house. On the stone above the bay, with the deer coming down, I understood that the voice had been saying one small thing all along, and that the dead, if they loved me, would not need me to follow them in order to prove I had heard. I will set the thing down here as the last sentence of this account, although I do not know how to set it down. It is not really a sentence. It is closer, perhaps, to a single word, although I have no single word for it. It is what the bay says, in the evenings, to anyone who will sit on a stone above it for an hour and not move. It is what the dog said, when he came to me at the roundabout south of Wigan, by the small movement of his head against my thigh. It is what the bee woman said, in the kitchen in Hawes, when she had laid her hand on his head. It is what my grandmother wrote when she told me to love the dead without following them. It is what the deer say, coming down to drink at the end of a day, by the act of coming down to drink.

It is: here.

Only that.

I rose from the stone in the dark. The dog rose with me. We went down to the cottage, where the smoke was still going up, and the fire was still in the range, and the kettle, when I came in, was almost on the boil. I made tea. I sat with the dog by the fire. The cottage settled quietly around me. The night came on. The wind off the sea began to rise. I would write, perhaps, in the long winter, if I felt like it, the rest of what had happened to me, and what I had been. Or I would not. Either way, I was here. The dog was here. The bay was here, with the tide coming in below. The deer were here, in their wood, on the hill, doing their own work, which was older than mine, and would last longer.

It was enough.

It had always been enough.

I had only been, for a long while, somewhere else.

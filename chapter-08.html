Northbound

Chapter Eight

The Priest

The church stood by itself in a fold of low ground a mile from any village, with a small graveyard behind a yew hedge and a path of pressed earth running up to the door. There was no road to it, only the path. We came down to it across a field, drawn by the small grey tower above the trees, and as we drew nearer I saw that there was smoke coming out of a chimney at the back of the rectory beside it, a thin clean column rising straight up in the still afternoon.

The dog stopped at the gate of the graveyard. He sat down. He had not refused a place in this way since the library and I waited a little to see what he would do. He looked at the door of the church. He looked at the rectory. He yawned. Then he rose and walked slowly through the gate at my heel, with the air of a creature who has overruled himself and would prefer not to be questioned about it.

The path ran between the church and the rectory and at the back I saw a small kitchen garden behind a low wall, and a man kneeling in it, weeding. He had not heard us come. I stood and watched him for a moment. He was old. His back was bent in a particular way that I have come to associate with men who have spent their lives over books or over altars or over both. He had a long-handled trowel in one hand and was using it without urgency. He was talking to himself, or perhaps to the weeds. I could not hear the words.

I cleared my throat. He looked up. He looked at me without surprise, although there was something in his face that the bee woman had not had, a small private warming, as though he were glad to see another human shape in the kitchen garden, although he had not been waiting for one. He set the trowel down. He stood up slowly, leaning on the wall. He brushed the earth from his hands.

"You are very welcome," he said. "Come round."

· · ·

His name was Father Edmund, although he asked me not to use the title. "I have not been a father in some long while," he said, leading me into the rectory kitchen. "I am only Edmund now. Edmund will do." The kitchen was small and low and dim. There was a deal table and three chairs. There was a pot on a small wood stove with something in it that smelled of barley and onion. There was a cat on a windowsill which had not bothered to look up at our coming and which the dog regarded with a single long doubtful glance and then ignored, as though they had agreed in some prior agreement not to make a scene of one another.

Edmund served me a bowl of the barley broth without asking whether I wanted it. He set a piece of dark bread beside it. He set a smaller bowl on the floor for the dog and the dog ate from it after looking at me to be sure he was permitted. We did not speak while we ate. Edmund ate slowly and chewed each spoonful for a long time, the way a man eats who has trained himself to it because his body no longer takes nourishment easily. When I had finished he asked me whether I would like another bowl and I said no, although I would have liked another bowl. He nodded as though I had answered correctly.

"Where are you walking," he said.

"North," I said.

"Yes," he said. "But where."

I told him. I told him about the loch and the island and the cottage and the deer. I had not told anyone these things before. I had not told the bee woman. I had not told the cataloguer. I was not sure why I was telling him. Perhaps it was that he had asked twice. Perhaps it was that he had eaten so slowly and chewed so long. He listened without speaking. When I had finished he nodded once and said only, "That is a good thing to walk toward. I am glad you are walking toward it."

"I do not know if it is still there."

"That is not the part of it that matters," he said.

I waited.

"What matters is that you have a place in your mind that you are walking toward," he said. "The mind needs such a place. The body needs such a place. Whether the place is there in the world is a smaller question than people think. The walking shapes you. You will be one shape if you walk toward the loch and another shape if you do not. The shape of you when you arrive at the shore, if you arrive, will be the answer the walking has made of you. Whether the cottage is standing or not standing is the world's answer, and it is a smaller answer."

I looked at him. He had not raised his voice. He had spoken in the soft quick way of a man who had said many such things in his time and who had stopped believing he would persuade anyone, and so was free to say them as though he were simply naming objects in the room.

"You were a priest," I said.

"I was."

"You are not now."

"I am not."

"Why not."

He looked at me for a moment. Then he smiled, with a dry small mouth, and pushed his bowl an inch away from him on the table.

"You will want to know," he said, "and I will tell you, although I have not told it to anyone before. There has not been anyone to tell. I had been a priest for forty-one years. I had been a good priest, in the way one is good at a thing one has loved badly and well, and I had said the offices and given the sacraments and visited the dying and married the young, and I had believed, in my way, although it had been a thinner believing in the last years than it had been in the first. The thinness did not trouble me. I had been told it would come. I had read about it in books. I had said to myself, this is the dark night that they wrote of, this is the small price of a long obedience, and I had gone on saying the offices."

He paused. He looked at his hands on the table.

"Then the thing came," he said. "I do not need to tell you. You have heard. It came in below the hearing. I felt it in the church. I had been at the altar. I had been about to lift the bread. The bread was in my hands. I felt it come in, the way you feel a deep note in your sternum, and I stood at the altar with the bread in my hands and I listened to it. There was no one in the church but me. I had been alone with the bread and the chalice. I stood there. It went on for a long time. I do not know how long. Perhaps an hour. Perhaps less. It felt like a great deal more."

"And then?"

"And then it went," he said. "And I set the bread down. And I went out of the church and I sat on the bench under the yew tree and I waited for what I would do next. I had thought I would weep. I had thought I would pray. I did neither. I sat under the yew. After some long while I knew what I was. I was a man who had been carrying a weight for forty-one years which was not his weight to carry. I knew it the way you know a stone has gone out of your shoe. The shoe was the same shoe. The foot was the same foot. The walking was the same walking. There was simply no longer the stone."

"What was the weight."

"The need to make sense of it," he said. "The need to put a face on the great thing. The need to say God when I meant that which I cannot say. The weight was the word, perhaps. Or it was the using of the word as though it were a hand and not a hole. I do not know. I was not a clever man. I am only telling you what it felt like in the leaving of it."

"You were freed."

"Yes," he said. "Although I would not say it that way. To say I was freed makes a story of it. I was simply lighter. I sat under the yew and I was lighter, and I looked at the yew, and I looked at the grass, and I looked at the stones in the wall, and I looked at them in a way I had not looked at them in forty-one years. I had been looking at them as evidence. I had been looking at them as small witnesses to the great thing. Now I was looking at them only as themselves. The yew was the yew. The grass was the grass. The stones were the stones. They were enough."

· · ·

He took me into the church afterwards. I do not know why I went. The dog stayed in the kitchen with the cat and the smell of the broth, and Edmund and I crossed the path between the buildings and went in by the small low door at the side. The church was cold and dim and smelled of old stone. The pews stood in their rows. The altar was bare. The cloths and the vessels had been taken away and laid I do not know where. There was a single candlestick of brass on a side table. There was no candle in it. There was the smell, very faint, that all churches have, although I could not have said what it was the smell of. The world inside a church is its own world.

"I do not come in much now," he said. "I keep the garden. I keep the fire in the rectory. I bake on Tuesdays. I read in the evenings. The church I leave to itself. It is its own thing. It does not require me. I had been told for forty-one years that it required me, and it did not. It is good to know what does not require you. It is better to know what does."

"What does," I said.

"The bread, when I make it," he said. "The garden, when I tend it. The fire, on a cold night. The cat, when she is hungry. The few small things I can hold in my hands. They are not the great thing. There is no longer a great thing. There are only the small things and the attention I bring to them. It is enough. It is more than I had thought possible, when I was busy with the great thing."

I stood in the dim church and looked at the bare altar and thought about the building of glass. I had been busy with the great thing for nineteen years, although I had not called it that and had not known its name. I had thought I was busy with the small things. I had thought the small things were the work. I had not understood until that moment that the small things, in the building of glass, were only the great thing wearing a small thing's clothes.

"Edmund," I said.

"Yes."

"Did anyone come back. After the thing. Did anyone come back to the church."

He looked at me. He smiled the dry small smile.

"You came back," he said.

· · ·

I stayed that night in the rectory. He gave me a small upper room with a single bed and a window that looked out onto the kitchen garden and beyond it into the field where I had come down, and I lay in the bed and looked at the ceiling and listened to him moving about below in the kitchen, and the cat going down the stairs, and the small clear sounds of a house with one person in it who has lived alone for a long time and has worked out where to put his feet so that the boards do not creak.

Before I slept I heard him in the room below me say a few words quietly to himself. I could not hear them. They had the cadence of an old form. He had said he no longer believed. I thought of him saying his old prayers anyway, in the kitchen, in the dark, to no one, and the thought did not sadden me. It was a kind thought. He had sat under the yew and the stone had gone out of his shoe, but the foot was still the foot, and the walking was still the walking, and a man who has said the same words at evening for forty-one years does not stop saying them only because he has stopped meaning by them what he used to mean. They were now a different shape of words. They were older, perhaps. They were quieter. They were his.

I slept a long full sleep, and dreamed of the figure on the ridge.

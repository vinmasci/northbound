Northbound

Chapter Twenty-Three

The Cottage

The door was not locked. There was no lock on it. There was a wooden latch, the old kind, a peg through a slot, that lifted when one pressed a small thumb-piece on the inside, and when one pulled the door from the outside the peg came up and the door opened. The door opened easily. The hinges had been oiled. Someone had been to the cottage in the last few months. I did not know yet who.

I stood on the step for some long time before I went in. The dog stood beside me. The wind off the sea moved my hair and his coat. I had walked a hundred and seventy days for this door. I had not, until I reached the step, allowed myself to picture the door, or what would lie beyond it. I had not wanted to crowd the door with my picturing. I had wanted to come to it as it was. I had succeeded in this in a way that surprised me. I had thought the door would be larger. I had thought it would be more painted. I had thought there would be something of significance about it. The door was a low door, painted black, with the paint peeling at the bottom, and a wooden latch. It was a door.

I lifted the latch. I went in.

· · ·

The room was small. There was a single room downstairs, with a stone-flagged floor, and a small range against the inside wall, and a deep window seat under each of the two front windows, and a pine table in the middle with two wooden chairs at it, and a small dresser against the back wall with a few plates and cups on its shelves. There was a narrow staircase against the far wall going up to whatever was above. The walls were whitewashed and the whitewash had been done not long ago. The flags had been swept. There was kindling and split logs in a basket by the range. There was a kettle on the range, cold but full of water, and a tin teapot with a knitted cosy on it, also cold, sitting on a tile beside the range. There was a small posy of sea-pinks in a stoneware jug on the table, the flowers withered to a soft brown but still in their shape, the water in the jug long gone.

I stood inside the door for a long while. The dog stood beside me. He sniffed the air carefully. He did not seem worried. He walked, after a moment, to the range and lay down in front of it on the warm stone, although the range was cold. The stone perhaps held a memory of warmth. The dog perhaps could feel things I could not. I did not interfere with him.

I went to the table. I sat in one of the chairs. I put my pack on the floor. I laid my hands on the wood of the table.

I had a feeling I cannot give you the right name for. It was not the feeling of being home. I had not been here before. I had only heard of the place from a woman who had been gone twenty years. It was not the feeling of arrival, in the sense of a thing being concluded. The thing was not concluded. It was the feeling, perhaps, of being expected. The cottage had been expecting me. It had not been preparing for my arrival in the way of a person preparing a meal. It had been doing what it always did, in its own quiet way, and would have been doing it whether I came or not, and was doing it still, with me sitting at the table. The cottage was a continuing thing. I had walked into the middle of its continuing.

I sat at the table for some long time. The wind moved at the window. The dog breathed in front of the cold range. The light from the window in the front wall lay in a long pale square on the flags.

· · ·

After a while I rose. I went to the dresser. There were six plates, six cups, three bowls, a small jug, a pewter spoon. The plates and cups were old. They were not the kind of crockery one would put in a holiday cottage. They were the crockery of a person who had lived in the cottage for many years. The jug was the same shape as the jug on the table. They were a pair, I thought. The other was on the table holding the dead sea-pinks.

I went to the range. There was a small wooden box at the side with a lid. I lifted the lid. Inside there were spent matches, a dozen perhaps, and an empty packet of new matches with two unused matches still in it, and a small twist of paper. The paper was a list. The list was in pencil, in a hand I did not recognise. It said:

flour — running low
salt
candle stubs — do not throw, can be melted
peat: take from the cut by the south wall, NOT the new face
door hinge: oil before winter
Donal not until early spring — loch sets in dec

I held the list. I read it three times.

I had not wept since the glen. I did not weep now. I felt only a small shifting in my chest, the kind of small shifting one feels when a thing one had thought was empty turns out to have something in it after all. The list was the thing the cottage had been doing. The cottage had not been waiting for me. The cottage had been being lived in. Recently. Within months. By a person whose hand was not my grandmother's hand, although I had not seen my grandmother's hand since I was seventeen and could not now have sworn to her hand if I had been shown it.

I put the list back in the box. I closed the lid.

"Hello," I said quietly.

There was no answer. The dog raised his head and looked at me. He laid it back down on his paws.

· · ·

I went up the staircase. The stairs were narrow and the treads were old and one of them creaked under my foot in the middle. The upper room was a single low room under the slope of the slate roof, with a small window in the gable that looked out over the bay to the west. There was a single bed under the window, made up neatly with a grey wool blanket folded across the foot, and a small chest of drawers against one wall, and a chair beside the bed. There was a book on the chair. There was a candlestick on the chest of drawers with a part-burned candle in it.

The book on the chair was an old, much-handled copy of the Bible, but small, the kind people used to carry, with a black cloth cover gone shiny at the edges. There was a strip of green ribbon tucked into it as a marker. I lifted the book. It opened at the place of the ribbon. I read a few of the lines my eye fell on. They were from a psalm. I have calmed and quieted my soul, like a weaned child with its mother; like a weaned child is my soul within me. I closed the book and put it back on the chair as I had found it.

I went to the chest of drawers. I did not open the drawers. I felt I had not yet earned the drawers. I went to the window.

The window in the gable looked out west over the small bay. The tide had come in a little since I had landed at the inlet on the other side of the island, and the bay below the cottage was now half full, the water moving in slow over the rocks, the rocks dark and wet. The sun was on the water. There was a single seal on a rock at the far side of the bay, lying like a long dark cigar in the sun. There were no other creatures visible.

I stood at the window a long while. I looked down at the bay. I looked at the seal on the rock. I looked at the long line of the open sea beyond the bay, and the small distant shapes of further islands on the horizon, and the cloud lying along them.

I had walked all this way to stand at this window.

I had not, until that moment, understood that this was what I had walked all this way for. I had thought I had been walking for a place. I had been walking, perhaps, for a window. Or for the looking out of a window. Or for what one looks at, in the morning, from a window in a small upstairs room in a cottage on an island, when one has nothing else one has to be doing, and one's dog is asleep below on the warm stone, and the sea is doing its slow long work in the bay, and a seal is on a rock, and the wind is in the gable, and one is, finally, where one is.

The bay below me filled slowly with the tide.

· · ·

I went back down the stairs. The dog lifted his head. I went to the range. I knelt beside it. I opened the small door at the front. There was a bed of cold grey ash inside, and on top of the ash a few pieces of charred wood, and the smell of old peat, very faint. I cleared the ash carefully into a metal bucket I found in the corner. I laid a fire of kindling and split logs and a small chunk of peat from the basket. I struck one of the matches from the box. I lit the kindling. The fire took.

I closed the door. The fire began to roar quietly behind it. I sat down in front of the range on the floor, beside the dog, with my back against the warm stone of the hearth.

I did not yet know who had been here. I did not yet know where they had gone. I did not know whether they would come back. I did not know whether the cottage had been kept, all these years, by my grandmother herself, who I had been told had died, but who perhaps had not died in the way I had been told, or by someone else, or by a succession of someone elses, the way Iona had said the list was held among the refusers, passed and tended, place to place. I did not know any of this.

I knew only that I was in the cottage, and the cottage had been being lived in, and the fire was lit, and the dog was warm, and there was a window upstairs that gave out onto a bay where seals lay on rocks. There was time. There would be time to know. There would be time to find out what the cottage held. There were drawers I had not opened. There was a small shed I had not gone into. There was the rest of the island I had not walked. There were perhaps things that the person who had been here had left for whoever came next, and I was the next. I would find them in their own time.

I sat on the warm flags by the dog and I watched the small fire through the gap at the edge of the door, and the cottage settled quietly around me, and it accepted me with no fanfare at all, in the way the cottage had accepted, perhaps, many before me, on many previous quiet afternoons. I was not the first. I would not be the last. I was only, today, the one who had come in.

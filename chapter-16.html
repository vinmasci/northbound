Northbound

Chapter Sixteen

The Carry

The dog began to limp on the morning of the fifth day after I left Iona. I had not noticed at first. He had been walking ahead of me along a forest road through Galloway and had perhaps been favouring the left foreleg for some time before it became clear, hiding it the way a working dog hides such things, but at a certain point he stopped walking ahead of me and dropped back to my heel and walked there, which was unlike him. I looked down. He looked up. He gave me a small careful look that admitted nothing.

I stopped. I crouched. He sat. I lifted the foreleg. There was a small cut on the pad, perhaps an inch long, with grit pressed into it. The pad was hot. The leg above had begun to swell.

"Oh," I said quietly. "Oh, lad."

He set his nose against my cheek for a moment. I do not know what he meant by it. I think he meant that he had not wanted to trouble me, but that the trouble had got beyond what he could carry on his own.

· · ·

I cleaned the cut with water from my flask. I washed the grit out as best I could. I had a small tin of beeswax balm that the bee woman had given me. I had not used it. I rubbed a little into the pad. I cut a piece of cloth from the bottom of my shirt with Iona's knife and bound the foot, although I knew the bandage would not last an hour on a walking dog. He bore all of this with a stillness that almost broke me. He kept his face turned a little aside, not looking at the foot, the way a child will not look at a needle being put into the back of his hand. When I had finished he licked the back of my wrist once, very briefly, and stood up. He took two steps and the leg gave under him. He did not whimper. He looked back at me and waited.

"Right," I said. "Right then."

I sat down on a stump by the side of the track. I took the pack off my back. I went through it slowly. I had not had occasion to consider what I would carry in another shape than this, the long shape of the pack on the long line of the road. I had had room only for what I would need myself. I had not had room for a creature.

I took out the two heavy things first. I put them on the ground. The book. The jar of honey, half-full. I put them on the ground beside the pack. Then I took out the rest in order, slowly, looking at each: the bread that Iona had given me, the cheese, the apples in their cloth. The kindling I had been carrying for fires. A spare pair of socks. The strip of oiled cloth with the knife. The packet of porridge oats. The tin mug. The small green book. A folded square of canvas. The two-litre water bottle. The torch with its weak yellow light. A roll of waxed thread. A box of matches in a tin.

I laid them out in the moss along the edge of the track and I considered each.

I left the food. I left the matches. I left the knife. I left the water bottle. I left the small green book.

I left the rest by the side of the track in a careful pile against the trunk of a sitka, the way a person leaves a thing for someone else to find, although there was nobody to find it. I did not feel I was throwing things away. I felt I was returning them to a country that had given them to me in the first place. I balled up the empty pack and stuffed the food and the book and the bottle and the matches and the knife and the porridge into a tighter shape inside it. There was room. There was a great deal of room. I had been carrying air, mostly. I had not known.

I took off my coat and rolled it and tied it across the top of the pack. I took the canvas square and folded it. I came back to the dog.

"All right," I said. "All right then."

He let me lift him. He weighed perhaps thirty-five pounds, which is more dog than I had thought. I had not lifted him before. He had the long-boned heaviness of a creature whose ribs you can see and whose body is mostly purposeful weight. I sat back on the stump with him in my lap and arranged the canvas across my chest. I made a sling of it, looping it round under him and up over my shoulders, the way I had seen women carry small children in the long-ago villages I had read about. I tied the corners across my back. I stood up. He hung against my chest with his back against my collar bones and his hindquarters resting in the crook of my left arm. His head was under my chin. He looked very gravely at the country going past as I took my first steps.

"You are heavier than I thought," I said. "You will need to do this for both of us."

He laid his head back against my throat. We walked.

· · ·

I carried him for two days. We did not go fast. We did not go far. The first day I made perhaps eight miles, although the ground was not difficult. The second day I made six. By the second evening I had begun to understand something of the pace at which the world had moved before we had filled it with engines. The pace was the pace of a person carrying a creature, or a child, or a load of fodder, or a sack of grain. It was a slow pace. It was a pace that gave you time to look at every grass-head along the side of the road. It was a pace in which a single wood took a half-day to walk through, and the half-day was not lost, was indeed perhaps the only time in your life when you saw a wood.

I had walked, before this, fast. I had thought I had been walking slowly because I was no longer riding in trains. I had not been walking slowly. I had been walking at the pace of a man with a pack and a road and an idea about a place beyond the horizon. I had not been walking at the pace of a creature carrying a creature. The carrying was a different thing entirely. The carrying did not let you forget what you were doing for an instant. The carrying said, the whole hour, this dog, this dog, this dog.

The dog slept against my chest for some part of each day. He woke now and again and turned his head and looked at the country. He did not whine. He did not shift to be more comfortable than he was. He took the carry the way he had taken the cut: as a thing the world had given him, which was to be borne with attention and without complaint. I would not have been able to do this. I did not know any human who would have been able to do this.

"Teach me," I said to him under my breath in the dim afternoon under the sitkas. "Teach me how you do this."

I do not think he heard. He was asleep against my collarbone. His ribs were going gently in and out. The track went on.

· · ·

On the second night I camped by a beck and washed the cut again. The swelling was down. The pad had begun to close. I made a small fire and ate some of the cheese and a piece of bread and gave him bread soaked in the warm water from the kettle, which he ate from my hand. He licked my fingers when he had finished. He looked into the fire. He looked at me. He looked into the fire again.

"Tomorrow," I said.

I let him sleep against me by the fire. He slept in a different way that night. He slept the way a creature sleeps who knows he has been carried. It is a particular sleep. It is heavier than the ordinary sleep. It is a sleep that allows itself to be cared for, which is not the same as the sleep of a working dog. I think he had not slept that sleep in a long time, perhaps not since he was a pup. I did not know whether he had ever been a pup that someone had carried. I hoped he had. I sat by the fire with my hand on his flank and I hoped, on his behalf, retrospectively, that there had been someone who had carried him before me.

I thought, with my hand on his flank and the fire going down, of all the carrying that had been going on in the world before I had begun to notice. The bee woman had been carrying her bees. The cataloguer his books. Edmund his garden and his cat. Walenty his cows. Iona her firth in the early morning. The world had been full of things being carried by careful people, and I had been in a building of glass not carrying anything, and not being carried, and not noticing. The not-carrying had been the trouble. I had not understood until I had a hot pad and a swollen leg in my arms. The trouble was that nothing in the building of glass had ever needed me to lift it. Nothing had ever asked. I had thought that was freedom. I had not understood that it was the opposite of freedom. I had understood it now. I would not forget.

· · ·

On the third day he wanted down. I felt him move in the sling, a small precise movement, and I stopped. I knelt and untied the canvas. He stood on his own. He held the foreleg lightly off the ground. He took a step. He took another. He looked back at me. He took a third. He set the foreleg down. He put his weight on it. He did not flinch.

I crouched. I put my forehead against his forehead. He stood very still under it for a moment, the way he sometimes did. Then he licked the corner of my mouth and stepped back and trotted ahead of me up the track.

I watched him go a few yards. He looked back. He waited.

"Yes," I said. "All right. I am coming."

I shouldered the pack. It was a third of its old weight. I felt almost as though I had no pack at all. I went after the dog up the track in the morning light, and the carrying had gone out of my arms and into some other place in me, and I did not know what to do with it yet, only that I would not forget where it was.

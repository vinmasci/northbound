Northbound

Chapter Seven

Storm

The storm came up out of the west on a morning when the sky had been clear at first light. We had walked a long quiet hour up a lane between hawthorn hedges and it had seemed it would be a good day for walking. Then the clouds began to come over the shoulder of the fell ahead of us, the colour of slate and slow as cattle, and the air dropped its temperature in the way it does when something is on its way that the world is preparing for. The dog had felt it before I had. He had begun to look up, and back, and to shake himself for no reason. I had learned to trust his attention before my own, and so I began to look for a place.

There were no buildings on the lane. There were stone walls and there were hedges and there were small wet fields of bracken running up to the open fell, and there was nothing else. I went on for half an hour with the wind beginning to come at us in long unsteady pulses and the first hard drops striking my hood, and at last, where a track turned off the lane and went up between two beech trees, I saw a low building set back in the field above. A barn, I thought, or a shippon, or what they call up here a bank-barn, the kind built into the hill so the upper floor opens at one level and the lower at another. We turned off the lane and went up the track at a half-run, the dog a pale streak ahead of me. The rain came down in earnest as we reached it.

The lower door was off its hinges and lay against the wall. We went in. The air inside smelled of old hay and stone and the dust that lives in such places. There were no animals. There had perhaps not been animals for some years before the leaving had even begun. The far end of the barn had a partition of weathered planks and behind the partition a heap of loose hay perhaps three feet deep. I went round and pulled an armful of it out and made a place against the wall where the floor was driest, and we sat down in it, the dog and I, and listened to the storm break over the roof.

· · ·

It was a real storm, the kind that comes off the sea and has been given the whole open run of the country to gather speed. The rain on the slate roof was a continuous drumming. The wind found its way under the eaves and thrummed in the timbers above us and rattled the planks of the partition. Twice, things outside that had been forgotten by their owners came down with sharp percussive noises in the yard, a sheet of corrugated iron perhaps, and once what sounded like a tree, although I did not go out to look. The dog pressed himself against my side. He shivered now and again, although it was not cold inside the barn. He shivered the way a creature shivers who has not yet decided whether to be afraid.

I put my arm round him. I had not been a person who put his arm round things, in the building of glass. I had been a person who shook hands and clapped people on the shoulder and meant nothing by either. But the dog had taught me, slowly and without instruction, the thing about putting an arm round a creature whose ribs you can feel through its coat. I put my arm round him and he settled, as he always settled, and his shivering eased.

"It is only weather," I said to him. "It is only weather and we are inside of it."

I do not know what he understood of the words. He understood the tone. He understood the arm. He understood that I was attending to him. These are the things a creature needs to know.

· · ·

I had the small green book in my pack and I took it out. The Living Mountain. I opened it at the beginning. The light coming in through the small window at the end of the barn was poor but there was enough of it to read by, and I read perhaps twenty pages slowly while the storm went on overhead. The book was about a woman who walked in the mountains for many years and who had, by the end of the walking, come to believe that the mountain was not something she went up but something she went into. She did not say this in the way I am saying it. She said it more carefully than that.

I put the book down at last and looked at the planks of the partition and the rain coming through a small gap in them where it had not before, and I thought about her sentence. Into and not up. I thought about my own walking. I had been walking up the country, north and north, on a road that I imagined as a kind of long line drawn on a map. I had been measuring my walking by miles and by days. I had been moving through the country as a thing one moves through, the way one moves through an airport, on the way from somewhere to somewhere else.

But the bee woman had said that walking was a thing you knew where you were walking to, or you were not really walking. The cataloguer had said that the work was the thing, and the readers had been a gift. The two of them together, and the woman in the small green book, were perhaps saying the same thing to me in three different ways. They were saying: the walking is also the place. The walking is also the work. You will arrive when you have walked far enough into the walking, and not before. The road is not a line. The road is a country, and you are in the country, and the country is in you.

I did not know what to do with this. I am a man who has, for most of his life, mistaken motion for arrival. I had been moving since I was nineteen and I had not arrived anywhere, although I had been told many times that I had. I had been told that I had arrived when I had got the job, and I had been told that I had arrived when I had got the flat, and I had been told that I had arrived when I had got the woman, although by then she was already getting ready to leave, and I had not noticed because I was busy arriving. I had not arrived. I had only moved.

Now I was walking up a country in which there were almost no people, with a dog who had no name, toward a house I half remembered, and I was beginning to suspect that I was, perhaps for the first time, really walking. Whether I would arrive I did not know. The bee woman would have said I would. The cataloguer would have said it did not matter. The woman in the green book would have said the question was wrong.

· · ·

The storm went on for some hours. I dozed against the wall of the barn with the dog's weight along my thigh and the rain a continuous sound above us, and when I woke at last the rain had eased and the wind had dropped and through the small window I could see that the light outside had a different quality, a thin clean grey, the way the light is after a storm has done its work and gone on to do it elsewhere.

I sat up. The dog raised his head. I rummaged in the pack for one of the bee woman's jars and broke off a piece of her bread and we ate the bread with honey from the lid of the jar in the dim air of the barn. The honey was the dark amber kind. It was thick and smelled of something I could not name, perhaps heather. I gave the dog the empty lid to lick. He licked it carefully. He licked it for a long time. He set it down on the floor when he was done.

I went out. The yard was full of small lakes. The corrugated iron sheet had come down from the roof of an outbuilding and lay buckled across the cobbles. Beyond the yard, beyond the wall, the fells rose into the grey sky and were lit in patches by the sun where it had broken through, and the country was so green it was almost painful to look at. A buzzard was up high above the nearest peak, riding a thermal that the storm had set running in the air behind it.

The dog came out and stood beside me. He looked at the world. He looked at me.

"All right," I said. "All right then."

We went back down the track to the lane and we walked on north, and the wet road shone, and the hedges dripped, and the air was so clean it had a kind of edge to it, and I walked and I thought about the woman who had walked into the mountain, and about the man who had bent his head over his ledger in the long room of the library, and about the woman with the bees in the lavender, and about the figure on the ridge who had not come down. I thought how each of them, in their own way, was teaching me a thing I had been trying to learn for a long time and had not had the right teachers for. I thought how teachers come, when they come, in the shape of what one is ready to receive. I thought how late my readiness had come, and how strange, that it had taken the emptying of a country to bring it on.

The dog ran ahead. He found a puddle and trotted through it and looked back at me as though to say that puddles, after a storm, were among the better things the world had on offer. I had not thought about this either. I was beginning to learn it. I went round the puddle, because I am a man and I had not yet given up all my old habits. The dog forgave me, as he always did. We walked on into the long bright afternoon.

Northbound

Chapter Nine

The Drawing

Two days north of Edmund's church I came to a school. It stood at the edge of a small village above a meadow that ran down to a stream, a low building of pebbledash and tile with a yard at the front and a row of beech trees marking the boundary. The gate was open. The front door was open. There was a coat hung on a peg in the porch, a child's coat, blue with a yellow toggle, and a pair of small black shoes set neatly side by side on the floor beneath it.

I went in.

I do not know what I was looking for. I had not gone into a school in any of my walking. I had a feeling about schools that I had not examined and so I had passed the others by. But this one had its door propped, and the dog had gone up the path before me, and I followed him because to refuse would have required an explanation I was not prepared to give myself.

The corridor inside smelled of nothing. That was the first thing I noticed. Other places I had been into had held some old odour of their use, the bread of a kitchen, the floor polish of a library, the cold incense of a church. This corridor was scrubbed clean of itself. There were small green lockers along one wall. There was a noticeboard with a few sheets of paper still pinned to it, curled at the corners. There was a row of pegs at child-height with no coats on them, except for the blue one in the porch, which had not been hung in this corridor.

I went down the corridor. The dog walked behind me with an unusual softness in his tread, the way he walked in a place he was not sure of. I came to a classroom on the left and I went in.

· · ·

The chairs were on the tables, upside down, the way chairs are put on tables when the day is over and the floor is to be swept. The board at the front had been cleaned. There was a date written in the top right corner in a child's careful hand, a Wednesday, although the month meant nothing to me now. Beneath the date someone had written, also in chalk, in a hand that was not the child's, the words Today we will think about, and then there was nothing. The sentence had not been finished. I stood and looked at it for a while.

The walls were covered in drawings. They were the usual wall of a primary classroom, although I had not been in such a room since I was seven and I had perhaps forgotten how busy the walls of children are. There were paintings of houses with smoke coming out of the chimneys. There were paintings of the sea with boats on it. There were paintings of families holding hands in a row. There were paintings of dogs and horses and cats, and one painting, somewhere along the back wall, of what looked like a giraffe, although it had been done by someone who had not yet quite met a giraffe in the flesh.

I walked along the wall slowly, looking at the drawings, and the dog followed me.

It was on the third wall that I began to notice the other ones.

· · ·

They were not paintings. They were drawings, in pencil or in coloured wax, on small sheets of paper that had been pinned up amongst the others. They were not the same as the others and the eye picked them out by a small lift of feeling before it knew what it was looking at. I stopped in front of one of them. Then I went a little further along and stopped in front of another. Then another.

They were all the same drawing.

That is not true. They were all of the same thing. The hands that had drawn them had been very different. Some had drawn it in pencil with the soft line a child of five makes who is still finding the relation between the hand and what it does. Some had drawn it in wax crayon in a single bright colour and the line was looped and shaky. Some had drawn it in biro, which seemed odd for a school until I saw that those ones were on the back of letters from someone called Mrs Hardacre about a school trip, and so had been done in haste, perhaps at home.

The thing they had drawn was a curve. It came down across the page from somewhere off the upper edge, a long arc descending, and it ended near the bottom of the page in a small further bend, like a hook, although a soft hook. There was nothing else on most of the pages. A few had a small house at the bottom, off to one side. A few had a tree. One had what might have been the figure of a person, very small, standing under the curve and looking up at it. All of them had the curve.

I counted them. There were nineteen on the third wall. I went back into the corridor and looked at the noticeboard. There was one there. I went into the next classroom along and there were eight more, scattered amongst the other drawings, all the same shape. I went into the staff room at the end of the corridor and on the cork board above the kettle there were three.

The staff room had a teacher's desk in it with a stack of unmarked exercise books on the corner and a mug with a dried ring of tea at the bottom. On the inside of one of the cupboard doors there was a piece of A3 paper pinned up. It was in an adult's handwriting. It said:

Reception class — ask the children to draw what they can hear.

I sat down on the edge of the teacher's desk. The dog came and sat at my feet and looked up at me.

· · ·

I had not heard anything. I had been listening, since the petrol station at Tebay. I had been listening, since the kitchen with the apple and the spoon. I had been listening, since the bee woman had said it came in below the hearing, and Edmund had said I felt it the way you feel a deep note in your sternum. I had been listening hard enough that I had stopped sleeping properly, in the second week, and had had to make myself stop listening a little or I would not have been able to walk.

I had not heard the curve.

The children had heard it. The children had heard it well enough that they had drawn it nineteen times in one classroom and eight in another and three in the staff room and one on the corridor noticeboard, and on the back of a letter from Mrs Hardacre about a school trip. The children had heard it well enough that the teacher had noticed they were drawing it, and had asked the reception class to draw what they could hear, and had pinned the instruction to the inside of a cupboard door so that she would remember to do it on the day. I did not know whether she had done it on the day. I did not know whether the children had drawn the curve again, or some other shape, or something they had not yet found a shape for. The teacher's desk was as it had been left. The exercise books were unmarked. The tea was a ring at the bottom of a mug.

I thought: the children heard it first. I thought: of course the children heard it first. The children had not had nineteen years in a building of glass to teach them to stop hearing.

I took down one of the curves from the cupboard door and folded it into the small green book the cataloguer had given me, between two of its pages, and I put the book back into my pack. I do not know why I took it. I had a feeling that I was being asked to carry a piece of evidence whose case had not yet been opened. The drawing was small and creased and the wax crayon had blue in it and a darker blue. The hand had been someone's, perhaps six years old. The curve came down and ended in its small soft hook.

· · ·

I went back into the corridor. The blue coat was still on its peg. The small black shoes were still on the floor below it. I stood in the porch and looked at them for a while. I did not pick them up. I did not move them. I did not want to be a person who moved a child's shoes from where they had been left. I went out and pulled the door to but did not shut it, the way I had found it, and the dog and I went down the path between the beech trees and out of the gate and down the road.

I walked for a long time without seeing what I walked through. I was thinking about the curve. I was thinking about the children. I was thinking about what it would be to be five years old and to hear a long descending note in a sky one had only just learned to look at, and to come into a classroom and to be given a piece of paper and a wax crayon, and to sit at a low table beside other small children, and to draw what one had heard. None of us, in the building of glass, would have drawn anything. We would have written a memo. We would have filed it under further information awaited. We would not have heard the note in the first place, because we had been busy not hearing notes for nineteen years, and you cannot stop being busy at a thing in a single morning, even if the world asks you to.

The children had not been busy. They had heard. They had drawn. The teacher had seen the drawing and had pinned an instruction to the cupboard door. The instruction had not been followed because the day on which it was to be followed had not come, or had come and gone, and a great many things had not been done that day.

I walked north with the curve in the green book in the pack on my back. The dog ran ahead and circled back. The road went on between hedges that were beginning, even now in the late summer, to think about turning. The light in the west had gone a colour I had no word for. I thought I might never have a word for it. I thought it might be enough to walk under it and not name it. The cataloguer would have said this. The bee woman would have said this. Edmund would have said it more quietly than either.

I walked, and I listened, and I did not yet hear anything, but I was listening differently now. I was listening as a child listens, with the whole face turned to the air. I would not have said I expected to hear it. I would have said only that, if it came, I would now perhaps have a chance.

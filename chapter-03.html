Northbound

Chapter Three

The Tally

It rained for two days after the village. We sheltered the first night in a cattle barn at the edge of a farm and the second under the slip-road bridge of a B-road that crossed a stream. The dog slept against my hip both nights and shivered in his dreams, his paws moving as though he were still walking, and I lay awake and listened to the rain and thought about the sentence in the kitchen. We were given the chance and most of us took it. I turned it in my mouth like a stone. I waited for it to give me an edge and it did not.

On the morning of the third day the rain stopped and we came out into a country washed clean and full of light. The road climbed a long shoulder of hill and at the top there was a layby and a single petrol station, low and white, with two pumps and a small shop behind a glass wall that had been broken at one corner and patched with a square of cardboard. Beyond the station the moor ran away in long folds toward the east.

I stood in the layby and looked at it for a while. The dog looked at it too. There were no cars at the pumps. The cardboard patch in the window had been done carefully, with the cardboard cut to fit and held in place with a strip of brown tape. Someone had wanted the wind kept out.

I went up to the door. The lock was broken but the handle worked. The bell over the door rang as I pushed it open and the sound went through me with a small shock, because it was the first bell I had heard in a long while and I had not known I was missing them.

The shop inside was tidy. The crisps were gone. The chocolate was gone. The tobacco was gone. What was left was the long shelves of tinned things at the back, and these had been left in something close to their original order. I went down the aisle. There were tins of soup and tins of beans and tins of fruit. I took two tins of pears and a tin of corned beef and a packet of cream crackers in their stiff paper. The dog had wandered behind the counter and was standing very still, staring at something on the floor. I went round to look.

It was a tin of pears, lying on its side, the paper label half torn off. The dog looked up at me with an expression of patient pride, as though he had been waiting some time to show me. I picked it up and turned it in my hand. It was unopened and intact and unaccountably warm, although that was only the sun coming through the south window onto the spot where it had lain. I put it in my pack with the others. The dog seemed satisfied.

It was then that I saw the wall.

· · ·

Behind the counter, on the wall beside the till, someone had been keeping count. There were tally marks made in pencil, the old way, four uprights and a bar across, and they ran in long even rows from the height of a person's shoulder down almost to the floor. I came round the counter and stood in front of them. The dog sat at my feet. There was no other sound in the building.

I counted by fives until I lost the thread and then I counted again and gave up on the number. There were a great many. Four hundred. Five hundred. More. The marks were neat at the top and grew slightly less neat further down, the way a man's hand will grow tired across many days, and then at a certain point became neat again, as though he had decided to attend to them, or had handed the work to someone else.

And then they stopped.

The marks ran down the wall and across, and across, and across, and at a certain row they simply ceased. There was no mark trailing off. There was no mark begun and abandoned. The last group was a complete five, the bar drawn cleanly through the four uprights, and after it the wall was empty.

I stood and looked at it for a long time.

I had seen a thing like this in school, very long ago, in a book about the war. Men in a cell had marked the wall and one day the marks had stopped, and the teacher had explained without explaining, and we had understood without understanding. But this was not a cell. This was a petrol station on a moor in the north of England, and the marks were in pencil, and someone had patched the broken window with cardboard and brown tape so that the wind would not come in.

I thought: he was counting until he was not counting. I thought: he stopped because the counting was no longer the work to be done. I thought of the sentence in the notebook in the village. We were given the chance and most of us took it. I thought: there is something I am circling. There is a shape forming that I cannot yet see.

The dog had risen and gone to the door. He stood there looking out into the layby. I went after him.

· · ·

Outside, a thrush had come down onto one of the petrol pumps and was singing. It was the loudest sound I had heard in some days. The thrush sang from the rounded shoulder of the pump as though the pump were a fencepost, the song rising and breaking and rising, and the dog and I stood in the doorway and did not move so as not to break it. The thrush sang for perhaps three minutes. Then it stopped, considered us, and went away over the moor.

I walked out into the layby. I sat down on the kerb beside one of the pumps. I took out a tin of pears and opened it with my knife and ate it slowly, sitting in the sun, and the dog sat beside me and ate the syrup out of the tin when I had finished the pears. The pears were sweet. They were almost intolerably sweet. I had not known until that morning that I had been hungry for sweetness.

I thought about the man with the pencil. I gave him a face, although I had no warrant to. I gave him a small face, a quiet man, perhaps the owner of the station, perhaps an attendant who had stayed on after there were no more cars to fill. I gave him a habit of careful attention. I gave him the patience to sit each morning with a sharpened pencil and put a single mark on a wall and then go about his day. And I gave him, at last, the moment when he had set the pencil down and not picked it up again, and had walked out the door, and had locked it behind him because the lock had not yet been broken when he left.

I did not know that any of this was true. I knew only that something in me wanted it to be. I wanted him to have left in his own time, by his own hand, with the small care of a man who had finished what he was given to finish.

· · ·

We slept that night in the petrol station. I did not want to. I had been keeping out of buildings since the village. But the moor wind had come up after dark and I did not want the dog to lie in it, and the shop was dry and the door closed, and I made a bed for us behind the counter out of two coats from a peg by the staffroom door and we lay together in the dim glow of the moon coming through the cardboarded window.

I lay on my back and looked up at the ceiling tiles, which were old and stained, and I thought about the wall behind me and the tallies upon it. I thought about all the small acts of counting in a person's life. Days until a holiday. Days until a child is born. Days until a wage. Days until a thing one has been waiting for is done. And I thought: the man with the pencil had been counting days until something. And then the something had come, and he had not needed to count any longer, and he had laid down his pencil and gone.

What had come, I did not yet know. But I felt that I had begun, in some part of me below the thinking, to know its shape. It was a shape I could not see. It moved at the edge of vision when I turned my head. It had been in the radio at Tebay. It had been in the kitchen in the village. It was here in the wall behind the counter. It was patient. It was waiting for me to look at it.

I did not look at it. I was not ready. I closed my eyes and I put my hand on the dog's flank, and I felt his breath go in and out, in and out, and I let his breath be the thing I attended to, and slept.
